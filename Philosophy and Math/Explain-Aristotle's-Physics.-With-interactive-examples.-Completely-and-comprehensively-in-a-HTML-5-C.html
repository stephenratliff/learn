1. Overview — The Study of Physis

For Aristotle (384–322 BC), physics (from physis, nature) was not about equations, but about understanding why things change.

His treatise Physics in eight books asks: What is nature? What is change (kinesis)? What makes motion possible? His answers dominated Western thought for nearly two thousand years.

Key distinction from modern physics: Aristotle explains the world in terms of purposes, qualities, and natural tendencies — not universal mathematical laws, forces, or atoms in a void. A stone falls not because of gravity, but because its nature is to seek its proper place at the center of the cosmos.

Physics in Eight Books

  • I–II Principles, nature, and the four causes
  • III–IV Change, the infinite, place, void, time
  • V–VI Kinds of change and continuity
  • VII–VIII Motion, the first mover

Nature, for Aristotle, is an inner principle of motion and rest. Artifacts have no such principle — they are moved from without.

2. Change & The Four Causes

All change is the actualization of potentiality. A block of marble is potentially a statue; the sculptor makes it actually a statue.

Aristotle identifies four kinds of change:

  • Substantial — coming-to-be and passing-away (a seed becomes a tree)
  • Qualitative — alteration (green to red, hot to cold)
  • Quantitative — growth and diminution
  • Local — change of place, or locomotion

3. Elements & Natural Place

The sublunary world (below the Moon) is made of four elements, defined by two pairs of contraries: hot/cold and wet/dry.

  • Earth — cold & dry — heaviest, moves down
  • Water — cold & wet
  • Air — hot & wet
  • Fire — hot & dry — lightest, moves up

The heavens are made of a perfect fifth element, aether, which moves naturally in circles forever.

Natural Place: Each element seeks its proper layer in the cosmos. Motion is not neutral; it is homecoming. Stop interfering, and things return where they belong.

4. Motion: Natural and Violent

Natural motion requires no continuous force — it follows the element's inner nature (stone down, fire up). Violent (forced) motion requires a constant pusher. Remove the pusher, motion stops.

Aristotle's Law of Motion

Speed is proportional to weight and inversely proportional to the resistance of the medium.

v ∝ (weight) / (density of medium)

No void can exist — in a void, resistance = 0, so speed would be infinite, which is absurd.

Projectiles: The Air-Push Theory

Aristotle struggled with thrown objects. His solution: the thrower moves the air, and the air rushes around to keep pushing the arrow from behind. Later thinkers like Philoponus proposed impetus — a quality impressed in the object itself.

5. The Cosmos: Spheres Within Spheres

Two Realms

Sublunary (Earth to Moon): realm of generation, corruption, and the four elements. Things change.

Superlunary (Moon outward): made of aether, perfect, eternal, moving in unceasing circles.

Beyond the outermost sphere is the Unmoved Mover — pure actuality, thinking itself, the final cause that draws all motion as love draws the lover, without itself moving.

6. Time, Place, Void & the Continuum

Place (topos) is not space, but the inner surrounding surface of a container — like a vessel holding water.

Time is “the number of change with respect to before and after.” No change, no time.

Continuum: magnitudes, time, and motion are infinitely divisible — not made of indivisible atoms. Between any two points, there is always another.

Why No Void?

  1. In a void, no directions (up/down) exist
  2. Speed would be infinite (v ∝ 1/0)
  3. No medium to transmit forced motion
  4. Bodies would not know where to rest

7. Legacy — Why It Lasted 2,000 Years

Aristotle's physics was comprehensive, commonsensical, and teleological — it explained the why, not just the how. It fit everyday experience: heavy things do fall faster (in air), flames do rise, rest seems natural.

Medieval Critiques

John Philoponus (6th c.): Dropped weights — they fell at nearly the same rate. Proposed impetus theory.

Avicenna & Averroes: Refined motion theory in the Islamic world.

Jean Buridan (14th c.): Developed impetus as a permanent impressed quality.

Galileo & Newton

Galileo's inclined planes showed acceleration is independent of weight (ignoring air resistance). Newton replaced natural place with inertia and universal gravitation: F=ma, not v∝W/R.

Yet Aristotle's questions — about causation, actuality, and the nature of change — remain alive in philosophy of science.
