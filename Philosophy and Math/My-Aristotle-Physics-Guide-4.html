Henri Bergson — Duration, Intuition, and the Philosophy of Time

From spatialized clocks to lived duration (durée réelle): an inquiry into why time is not a line but a melody.

1. Life and Context (1859–1941)

Henri-Louis Bergson was born in Paris on 18 October 1859 to a Polish-Jewish musician father and an English mother. A prodigy in mathematics at the Lycée Condorcet—he won the Concours Général in 1877 for a solution to a problem of Pascal—Bergson turned to philosophy at the École Normale Supérieure, convinced that the precision of mathematics missed the mobility of life. This double training shaped his entire project: to bring to philosophy a rigor equal to science, without accepting science’s habit of treating time as space.

His first major work, Essai sur les données immédiates de la conscience (1889), published in English as Time and Free Will, introduced the central distinction between measured time and real duration. It was followed by Matter and Memory (1896), Laughter (1900), and Creative Evolution (1907), the book that made him an international celebrity. By 1900 he held the chair of Greek and Latin philosophy at the Collège de France; his Friday lectures drew crowds into the street, where policemen regulated the queues.

Bergson’s influence was unusually broad. In America, William James called him a "genius" and found in durée a confirmation of his own "stream of thought." In literature, Marcel Proust attended his lectures (Bergson married Proust’s cousin) and transposed durée into involuntary memory; Virginia Woolf and James Joyce built novels from the interpenetration of moments. In philosophy, Maurice Merleau-Ponty, Alfred North Whitehead, and later Gilles Deleuze would return to him as the thinker of process.

He was awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1927 "in recognition of his rich and vitalizing ideas." Yet his reputation suffered after a famous debate with Albert Einstein at the Société française de philosophie on 6 April 1922. Bergson argued in Duration and Simultaneity (1922) that relativity theory confused a physicist’s measured simultaneity with the philosopher’s lived simultaneity. Einstein replied famously: "the time of the philosophers does not exist." The physics community sided with Einstein; Bergson retreated from public life.

He died in occupied Paris on 4 January 1941. Though the Vichy regime offered to exempt him from anti-Jewish statutes, Bergson refused, and stood in line for hours to register as a Jew. He is buried in Garches. His late work The Two Sources of Morality and Religion (1932) extended duration into ethics and mysticism.

2. Core Distinction: Time (temps) vs Duration (durée)

Bergson’s philosophy begins with a simple observation: the time we measure is not the time we live. Science, he argues, necessarily "spatializes" time. It lays moments out side by side like beads on a string, identical, countable, reversible. This is temps: homogeneous, quantitative, divisible into instants. A clock does not show time passing; it shows space traversed—hands moving over a dial, a pendulum swinging. We count simultaneities: "when the hand is here, the event occurs." We never perceive the interval itself.

Real time—durée réelle—is entirely different. It is heterogeneous, qualitative, and indivisible. It cannot be cut without changing its nature. Bergson asks us to listen to a melody. When you hear the first notes of a familiar tune, they are not gone; they linger, tinting the present note. The notes interpenetrate. If you stop the melody and isolate a single note, you have destroyed the melody. Duration is like that: a continuous flux in which past survives in the present, swelling, accumulating, like a snowball rolling downhill.

"Pure duration is the form which the succession of our conscious states assumes when our ego lets itself live, when it refrains from separating its present state from its former states... It is a succession without distinction, a mutual penetration." — Time and Free Will, 1889

Bergson distinguishes two kinds of multiplicity. Quantitative multiplicity is homogeneous: sheep in a field, units on a number line. You can count them because they do not change each other by being together. Qualitative multiplicity is heterogeneous: the shades of an emotion, the phases of a melody, the colors of a sunset merging. Each element borrows from the others; the whole is more than the sum.

This contrasts sharply with his predecessors. Immanuel Kant had argued that time is the a priori form of inner sense—necessary, universal, homogeneous. Bergson agrees that homogeneous time is a form, but says it is a form imposed by the intellect for action, not the deeper reality. And against Einstein, Bergson does not deny that physics needs a fourth coordinate t. He denies that t captures duration. t can be reversed in equations; durée cannot. Physics measures the length of the path; philosophy lives the walking.

The consequence is radical: our inner life is not a series of states but a becoming. When you recall your childhood, you do not bring back a photograph; you contract your whole past into a new present. That is why no two moments are identical: each carries the memory of all previous ones. Duration is memory inseparable from consciousness.

The Melody as Method

Return to the melody. Science would transcribe it as notes on a staff: C, E, G, each at a timestamp. That transcription is useful—you can replay it. But the lived melody is not points; it is the passage, the tension and release. Bergson insists philosophy must think like a musician listening, not like an engraver copying. This is why he will later demand a new method: intuition.

3. Intuition versus Intellect

Human intelligence, Bergson argues in Creative Evolution, evolved not to know reality as it is, but to act upon it. To act, we must make things solid, discrete, repeatable. The intellect therefore "spatializes": it cuts the flux into objects, freezes movement into snapshots, and projects them onto a homogeneous grid. Language reinforces this—nouns for things, verbs in tenses that treat time as a container.

The intellect is superb at geometry, engineering, and science. It builds bridges because it treats matter as divisible. But applied to life, consciousness, or time, it falsifies. It turns duration into a line and freedom into a calculation of motives.

Intuition is the countermovement. Bergson defines it not as vague feeling or instinct, but as "the sympathy by which one is transported into the interior of an object in order to coincide with what is unique in it and therefore inexpressible." To intuit duration is to place oneself back inside the flow, to think in time rather than about time. It requires effort—a reversal of habitual intellect, like learning to see the intervals between film frames rather than the projected motion.

"To philosophize means to reverse the normal direction of the workings of thought... to place oneself within the mobile reality." — Introduction to Metaphysics, 1903

His favorite image: knowing Paris. You can study maps, statistics, and photographs—invaluable for the intellect. Or you can walk its streets at dusk, feeling the continuity of neighborhoods. No map equals the walk, yet the walk cannot be communicated without freezing it into concepts. Philosophy must shuttle between the two, using concepts to point back toward intuition.

Thus Bergson does not reject science; he limits it. Science gives knowledge about reality from the outside. Metaphysics, by intuition, aims at knowledge from within.

4. Memory and Consciousness: Matter and Memory

In Matter and Memory (1896), Bergson reframes the mind-body problem through duration. He distinguishes two memories. Habit-memory is motor: your body learning to swim, to type, to recite a poem by rote. It is stored in nervous mechanisms, oriented to action, and lives in the present.

Pure memory is entirely different. It is the conservation of your whole personal past, not as files but as a virtual co-existence. Every moment you have lived survives, not diminished, but in varying degrees of tension with the present. Bergson depicts this with his famous cone (see Laboratory below).

Imagine an inverted cone, its base AB at the top, its apex S touching a horizontal plane P. P is the present—your body acting in the world. S is sensorimotor contact. The base AB is the totality of your past, coexisting. Between them are countless horizontal sections: memories more or less contracted. In ordinary action you are near S: you contract only the memories useful for the task. In dream, reverie, or deep recollection, you rise toward AB, relaxing the tension, letting distant memories flood in.

This solves a paradox: if the brain stored memories like a warehouse, brain damage would destroy them. But clinical cases show memories can return after trauma—they were not destroyed, only access was blocked. Pure memory is not in the brain; the brain is a "valve" or "switchboard" that selects which virtual memories become actual for action.

Crucially, duration is memory. "Consciousness means memory," Bergson writes. Without memory, there would be no continuity, only instantaneous states replaced by others—exactly the spatialized time of the clock. Because the past endures, the present is thick, and novelty is possible.

5. Élan Vital and Creative Evolution

Creative Evolution (1907) extends duration to life itself. Against both mechanistic Darwinism (life as chance + selection) and finalism (life unfolding a preordained plan), Bergson proposes the élan vital—vital impetus.

Life is not assembly of parts but a current that divides as it meets matter’s resistance, like a jet of steam condensing into droplets. Evolution creates genuinely unforeseen forms: the eye, instinct, intelligence are not predictable from earlier states, just as the melody’s next note cannot be deduced from previous ones. If you could rewind and replay evolution, it would differ.

"Time is invention or it is nothing at all." — Creative Evolution

Élan vital is not a mystical substance; it is the name for duration as creative. Matter tends to fall into repetition (entropy, habit). Life rides matter, inserting indetermination, memory, and choice. Intelligence spatializes to manipulate matter; instinct prolongs life’s movement directly (the wasp that paralyzes without anatomy lessons). Intuition in humans recovers that vital sympathy consciously.

Evolution, therefore, is not a ladder but a bush of divergent tendencies, each expressing duration differently. Humans retain the capacity to turn back on ourselves and grasp duration—hence philosophy, art, mysticism.

6. Free Will: Time and Free Will

The problem of freedom, Bergson claims, is a false problem created by spatialization. Determinists picture motives as weights on a scale; the strongest wins. Libertarians picture a self at a fork choosing between pre-existing paths. Both treat the self as a collection of states laid out in space, and time as the neutral arena.

In duration, the self is not a thing but a history. A decision is not selected; it matures. Think of an artist: she does not choose among pre-formed paintings; the work grows, and at a certain moment it is. The act expresses the whole personality at that moment, contracted.

Freedom, therefore, is rare and requires effort—the effort to re-enter duration and let the whole past act, rather than letting habits or social roles play automatically. Most actions are quasi-automatic, near the apex of the cone. Free acts are those in which we coincide with our durée.

"We are free when our acts emanate from our whole personality, when they express it, when they have that indefinable resemblance to it which one sometimes finds between the artist and his work." — Time and Free Will

7. Critique of Spatialized Time: Zeno, Cinema, Einstein

Three figures condense Bergson’s critique. First, Zeno of Elea. Achilles cannot catch the tortoise because the distance is infinitely divisible. Zeno treats motion as a line composed of points at rest. Bergson replies: motion is indivisible. You cannot reconstruct the act of moving from immobilities any more than you can reconstruct the melody from isolated notes. The paradox vanishes when you stop projecting space onto time.

Second, the cinematograph. In Creative Evolution, Bergson famously says the intellect is like cinema: it takes a series of instantaneous snapshots and re-projects them at speed, giving the illusion of movement. Science does this legitimately for prediction. Philosophy errs when it mistakes the film for reality. Real movement is not between frames; it is the indivisible transition.

Third, Einstein. In 1922, Bergson did not dispute the mathematics of relativity; he disputed its metaphysical interpretation. Physics needs multiple measured times coordinated by light signals. But lived simultaneity—two flows interpenetrating in a conscious perception—cannot be reduced to clock readings. Einstein insisted on a single, impersonal time of physics. Bergson insisted on the plurality of durations: yours, mine, the universe’s. Later physics would admit proper time for each worldline, ironically closer to Bergson.

The upshot: to understand time, do not count instants. Endure. Place attention on the qualitative change itself—how boredom stretches, joy contracts, grief deepens. That inner experience, not the coordinate t, is the root from which physics abstracts.

Bergson is often misread as anti-science. He explicitly admired science’s power. His point is methodological: analysis works by immobilizing. That yields knowledge of matter. But life, mind, and time require a different attention—one that follows mobility itself. Without it, we turn living problems into puzzles of arrangement.

Modern neuroscience of time perception echoes him: dopaminergic circuits dilate subjective duration under threat; memory load thickens the present; meditative states report merging of past and present. These are not clock errors but evidence that consciousness is temporal thickness.

9. GNU Octave Examples

Bergson eschewed mathematics for philosophy, but we can model his contrasts numerically. The scripts below are self-contained; paste into Octave or MATLAB.

1. duration_vs_discrete.m

% Simulate Bergsonian duration as continuous accumulation vs discrete sampling
% Duration accumulates qualitatively; clock samples instantaneous states

t = linspace(0,10,1000);                 % continuous lived time
duration = cumsum(sin(t).*exp(-t/10));    % past accumulates, decays slowly

t_clock = 0:1:10;                         % spatialized instants
clock = sin(t_clock);                     % isolated measurements

figure; hold on;
plot(t, duration, 'LineWidth', 2);       % lived, thick present
plot(t_clock, clock, 'o', 'MarkerSize', 8);
title('Durée (line) vs Temps (dots)');
xlabel('external parameter'); ylabel('experience / measure');
legend('continuous accumulation','discrete samples');
% Bergson: counting the dots loses the swelling curve between them

2. melody_interpenetration.m

% Show overlapping vs separated notes - melody destroyed by analysis
x = linspace(0,1,500);
n1 = exp(-((x-0.3)/0.1).^2);
n2 = exp(-((x-0.5)/0.1).^2);
n3 = exp(-((x-0.7)/0.1).^2);

figure;
subplot(2,1,1);
plot(x, n1+n2+n3, 'LineWidth',2);
title('Duration: interpenetrating notes (melody lives)');
ylabel('intensity');

subplot(2,1,2);
plot(x,n1,'LineWidth',1.5); hold on;
plot(x,n2,'LineWidth',1.5);
plot(x,n3,'LineWidth',1.5);
title('Spatialized: separated instants (melody killed)');
xlabel('time');

3. memory_cone.m

% Model past influence decaying toward present - Bergson's cone
past_distance = 0:0.1:10;                 % 0 = present, 10 = distant past
present_weight = exp(-past_distance/2); % recent past weighs more in action

% For pure memory (dream), weight flattens
pure_memory_weight = 0.7 + 0.3*exp(-past_distance/5);

figure; hold on;
plot(past_distance, present_weight, 'LineWidth',2);
plot(past_distance, pure_memory_weight, '--', 'LineWidth',2);
set(gca,'XDir','reverse'); % present on left like cone apex
xlabel('past distance (reversed)'); ylabel('influence on present');
title('Memory Cone: contraction for action vs expansion in recollection');
legend('action (apex)','dream (base)');

4. elan_vital_random_walk.m

% Deterministic vs creative evolution
steps = 500;
det = cumsum(ones(steps,1)*0.01);                 % mechanistic
creative = cumsum(0.01 + 0.05*randn(steps,1));   % élan vital: small indeterminations accumulate

figure; hold on;
plot(det, 'LineWidth',1.5);
plot(creative, 'LineWidth',1.5);
title('Élan vital: novelty through accumulated divergence');
xlabel('time steps'); ylabel('evolutionary change');
legend('deterministic','creative');
% Bergson: same impulse, but life inserts unforeseeable variation

10. Legacy and Why Bergson Matters Today

Bergson’s star dimmed after the 1930s under the rise of logical positivism and phenomenology’s critique, yet he never disappeared. Gilles Deleuze’s Bergsonism (1966) revived him, reading durée as the first theory of multiplicity and the virtual—concepts central to contemporary philosophy of difference, complexity, and digital culture. Merleau-Ponty borrowed the lived body as duration; Whitehead’s process philosophy echoes the primacy of becoming.

In literature and art, Bergson remains foundational. Proust’s madeleine is a textbook case of involuntary memory—past coexisting and flooding the present. Woolf’s Mrs Dalloway compresses a lifetime into a day because consciousness endures. Cinema itself, which Bergson criticized, later discovered montage as duration through Tarkovsky and Deleuze.

Today, his distinction is urgent. Artificial intelligence operates in clock cycles, processing discrete tokens, optimizing over spatialized datasets. It simulates the cinematograph perfectly. What it lacks, arguably, is durée—an accumulating past that qualitatively changes each new input, not by adding data but by transforming the whole. Debates about machine consciousness often replay Bergson vs Einstein: can measured time produce lived time?

Similarly, our culture of metrics—productivity apps, timestamps, lifelogs—treats lives as divisible instants. Burnout, attention fragmentation, and the feeling that "time speeds up" are symptoms of living in temps while starving durée. Bergson invites a practice: to recover intuition through art, meditation, or simply listening, allowing the past to thicken the present instead of being archived.

"The universe endures. The more we study the nature of time, the more we shall comprehend that duration means invention, the creation of forms, the continual elaboration of the absolutely new." — Creative Evolution

To read Bergson is not to abandon science but to remember its limits—and to reclaim the time that cannot be counted, only lived.
