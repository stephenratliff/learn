1. Life and Intellectual Context

Martin Heidegger was born on 26 September 1889 in Meßkirch, a small Catholic town in Baden. Trained initially for the priesthood, he studied theology and philosophy at Freiburg, where he encountered Aristotle, Scholasticism, Franz Brentano’s dissertation “On the Manifold Meaning of Being,” and above all Edmund Husserl’s Logical Investigations. The young Heidegger was less interested in doctrines than in a single, strangely neglected question: what does “Being” itself mean?

After service as a meteorologist in the First World War, Heidegger became Husserl’s assistant at Freiburg (1919–1923). His early lectures at Marburg (1923–1928) — on Aristotle, Augustine, Kant — drew students like Hans-Georg Gadamer, Hannah Arendt, and Herbert Marcuse. In 1927, at age 37, he published Sein und Zeit (Being and Time), dedicated to Husserl “in friendship and admiration.” The book announced a project to reawaken the Seinsfrage, the question of Being, which Heidegger claimed had been covered over since Plato and Aristotle turned Being into a highest genus or substance.

Heidegger succeeded Husserl to the chair at Freiburg in 1928. On 21 April 1933, a few months after Hitler’s appointment as chancellor, Heidegger was elected rector of Freiburg University. He joined the NSDAP on 1 May and gave the rectoral address, “The Self-Assertion of the German University,” invoking a spiritual mission for the German people. He resigned the rectorate in April 1934, disillusioned but never publicly recanting. After the war he was suspended from teaching until 1951.

This political entanglement is not peripheral gossip. It forces readers of Being and Time to ask whether its language of heritage, destiny, resoluteness, and the “they” can be separated from the historical decision Heidegger made. He spent the next four decades at his hut in Todtnauberg in the Black Forest, writing not systematic treatises but pathways, “ways to thinking.” He died in 1976. The 2014 publication of his Black Notebooks renewed controversy by revealing anti-Semitic and anti-modern passages interwoven with his critique of technology.

Nevertheless, the philosophical core remains. Heidegger’s project was not to add another theory of knowledge or ethics. It was to show that Western metaphysics from Plato through Nietzsche has asked about beings — things that are — while forgetting Being itself, the open clearing in which anything can show up as something at all.

Key dates: 1889 born; 1913 doctorate on psychologism; 1916 habilitation on Duns Scotus; 1927 Being and Time; 1933–34 rectorate; 1949 “Letter on Humanism”; 1976 death.

2. The Question of Being

Heidegger opens Being and Time with a quotation from Plato’s Sophist: “For manifestly you have long been aware of what you mean when you use the expression ‘being’. We, however, who used to think we understood it, have now become perplexed.” The perplexity is deliberate. We constantly say “is”: the sky is blue, the hammer is heavy, God is. Yet we cannot define “is” the way we define “blue” or “heavy.”

Traditional metaphysics, Heidegger argues, treats Being as the most universal concept, as actuality, as presence, as substance. It investigates Seiende — beings, entities — and assumes we already know what Being means. This is the ontological difference: Being (Sein) is not itself a being; it is that by virtue of which beings are intelligible. Confusing the two leads to what Heidegger calls the “forgetfulness of Being” (Seinsvergessenheit).

“The question of Being aims therefore at ascertaining the a priori conditions not only for the possibility of the sciences which examine beings... but also for the possibility of those ontologies themselves which are prior to the ontical sciences and which provide their foundations.”— Being and Time, §3 (Macquarrie & Robinson)

How to ask anew? Not by deduction. Heidegger adopts and transforms Husserl’s phenomenology: “to the things themselves.” But for Heidegger phenomena are not mental contents; they are what shows itself in itself. Crucially, much of what is most essential — Being, world, time — does not show itself directly; it withdraws while letting beings appear. Therefore phenomenology must become hermeneutic, interpretive. We must read the way Dasein already understands Being in its everyday dealings, and then carry out a Destruktion — a careful dismantling of the sedimented history of ontology from Aristotle to Descartes to Kant — not to destroy it but to loosen its coverings.

This method explains the strange language of Being and Time. Heidegger coins terms not to be obscure but to avoid importing 2,500 years of metaphysical baggage. He will not begin with “consciousness,” “subject,” or “human being,” because each already presupposes a split between inner mind and outer world. He needs a neutral name for the entity that can ask the question.

3. Dasein: The Entity That We Ourselves Are

Heidegger calls that entity Dasein, literally “being-there” (da = there, sein = being). Dasein is not “man” in biology, not “soul,” not “ego.” It is a formal indication for you and me insofar as we are the beings for whom our own Being is an issue.

“Dasein is an entity which does not just occur among other entities. Rather it is ontically distinguished by the fact that, in its very Being, that Being is an issue for it.”BT §4

This has three consequences. First, the “essence” of Dasein lies in its existence. Heidegger uses Existenz technically: Dasein is not a thing with properties; it stands out (ex-sistere) into possibilities. You are not first a finished object who then chooses; you are your possibilities — teacher or parent or refugee or thinker — which you take over, evade, or modify.

Second, Dasein is in each case mine (Jemeinigkeit). Even when I do what “one” does, it is I who must live and die it. Third, understanding of Being belongs to Dasein’s own constitution. We already move in a pre-ontological understanding of what it means for something to be: to be present, to be useful, to be alive.

Existentials vs Categories

Traditional ontology describes entities as present-at-hand with categories like quantity, quality, substance. Heidegger argues Dasein must be described with existentials — structures of existence. The three basic existentials are:

1. Understanding (Verstehen) — not intellectual knowledge but our practical ability to project upon possibilities. I understand a classroom because I know how to go on in it.

2. State-of-mind or disposedness (Befindlichkeit) — our always-already being attuned via mood. Moods are not inner feelings; they disclose how the world as a whole matters. Anxiety will become crucial.

3. Discourse (Rede) — the articulation of intelligibility in language, before sentences.

These are equiprimordial. Dasein is never worldless. Which leads to the most famous structure: being-in-the-world.

4. Being-in-the-World and the Two Ways Things Show Up

Heidegger hyphenates In-der-Welt-sein to block the picture of a subject inside a container-like world. World is not the sum of objects; it is the meaningful referential whole within which things count as something. A workshop world refers hammer to nails to wood to building to shelter to Dasein’s “for-the-sake-of-which.”

Ready-to-Hand vs Present-at-Hand

In our primary dealings, entities are not objects of detached observation. They are Zuhanden — ready-to-hand, handy, available for use. Heidegger’s famous example:

“The less we just stare at the hammer-Thing, and the more we seize hold of it and use it, the more primordial does our relationship to it become... Hammering does not have knowledge about the hammer’s character as equipment, but it has appropriated this equipment in a way which could not possibly be more suitable.”BT §15

When hammering flows, the hammer withdraws. It is transparent in use. It becomes conspicuous only when there is a breakdown: the head flies off (conspicuousness), the nail is missing (obtrusiveness), or something is in the way (obstinacy). In breakdown, the referential totality lights up. Only then do we stare and treat the hammer as merely Vorhanden — present-at-hand, an object with properties like weight and shape.

Science, for Heidegger, is a methodical making-present-at-hand. It is derivative, not primordial. The danger of Western thought is to take this theoretical stance as the primary way things are, thereby covering over the worldhood of the world.

Being-in-the-world is also being-with (Mitsein). Even alone, Dasein is constituted by reference to others. The everyday “who” is not myself but das Man — the “they,” the anonymous One who says what one does, thinks, feels.

5. Care (Sorge) as the Being of Dasein

After analysing everydayness, Heidegger asks: is there a unified structure of Dasein’s Being? He finds it in Sorge — care. Not worry, but the structural whole that makes concern possible.

“The Being of Dasein means ahead-of-itself-Being-already-in-(the-world) as Being-alongside (entities encountered within-the-world).”BT §41

This cumbersome formula names three moments:

Ahead-of-itself (Projection)

Dasein is always more than it factually is. It exists as Entwurf, projection upon possibilities. I am for-the-sake-of being a teacher, a parent, a thinker. This is the existential future.

Already-in (Thrownness)

Dasein is Geworfen, thrown. I did not choose my century, language, body, history. Thrownness is disclosed in mood — I find myself already in a world that matters. This is the existential past, what Heidegger calls having-been-ness (Gewesenheit).

Being-alongside (Fallenness)

Absorbed in everyday concerns, Dasein “falls” into the world of its dealings and into das Man. Not a fall from grace but the gravitational pull of averageness, curiosity, idle talk. This is the existential present.

Care unifies them: I project (future) from a thrown situation (past) while absorbed with entities (present). No piece comes first; they are equiprimordial. Only because Dasein is care can it encounter hammers as ready-to-hand or as broken objects.

6. Temporality — The Meaning of Care

The second division of Being and Time asks: what makes care possible? Answer: temporality. Not clock time, but original time that temporalizes itself.

Ordinary vs Primordial Time

Ordinary understanding, from Aristotle to now, treats time as a sequence of “nows” — points on a line. We measure it, waste it, save it. Heidegger argues this is derivative. It arises when Dasein, fallen into concern with present-at-hand things, levels off its own temporality and counts nows.

Primordial temporality is ecstatic and unified. Heidegger uses ekstatikon: standing outside itself. There are three ecstases:

1. The future — not a later now, but coming-toward-itself (Zukunft). Dasein exists as projection upon its ownmost possibility.

2. The having-been — not the past that is over, but the way Dasein comes back to its thrownness. I am as having been.

3. The present — not a now-point, but making-present, dwelling with what is at hand.

Crucially, the future has priority in Heidegger’s analysis of authentic temporality. Dasein first comes toward itself from its possibility, and only thereby can it retrieve its thrown past and engage the present.

“Temporality temporalizes itself as a future which makes present in the process of having been.”BT §65

Being-toward-Death

What possibility reveals temporality most primordially? Death. Not the biological event, but Dasein’s ownmost, non-relational, not-to-be-outstripped possibility.

Ownmost: death is mine alone; no one can die my dying for me. Non-relational: it individualizes Dasein, stripping away das Man. Not-to-be-outstripped: it cannot be avoided or mastered.

“Death is the possibility of the absolute impossibility of Dasein.”BT §50

When Dasein anticipates death — not morbidly waiting but running forward (vorlaufen) into it — it is wrenched from lostness in the They. The future no longer appears as infinite postponement but as finite. Thrownness is retrieved not as fate but as heritage. The present becomes moment of vision (Augenblick), where things matter urgently.

Thus authentic temporality is finite. Inauthentic temporality flees finitude, treats time as endless nows to fill, and lives from the present, forgetting death.

7. Authenticity, Anxiety, and the Call of Conscience

Authenticity (Eigentlichkeit) is not moral heroism. Eigen means own. Authentic Dasein owns its being; inauthentic Dasein is owned by das Man.

Das Man operates through distantiality (measuring oneself against others), averageness, and leveling down. It provides comfort: one judges, one enjoys, one shrugs. Heidegger lists its language: idle talk (Gerede), curiosity (Neugier), ambiguity.

Anxiety vs Fear

Fear has a definite object — a dog, a deadline. Anxiety (Angst) has no object. In anxiety, the world as significant whole slips away. Entities become irrelevant. Dasein is individualized and confronts itself as being-in-the-world as such.

“Anxiety reveals in Dasein its Being toward its ownmost potentiality-for-Being — that is, its Being-free for the freedom of choosing itself and taking hold of itself.”BT §40

The call of conscience interrupts idle talk. It says nothing specific, no moral rule; it calls Dasein from lostness back to its own guilt — not moral fault, but being-the-ground of a nullity, responsible for what it has not chosen yet must answer for.

Heidegger’s term for authentic existence is anticipatory resoluteness (vorlaufende Entschlossenheit): holding open the future (anticipation of death) while resolutely choosing in the moment, retrieving one’s heritage.

8. The Turn (Kehre) — From Dasein to Being

Being and Time was never completed. The planned second part, a destruction of Kant, Descartes, and Aristotle, appeared only in fragments. In the 1930s Heidegger spoke of a Kehre, a turn. It is not a rejection but a shift of emphasis: from the Being of Dasein toward Being itself as it gives itself historically.

Later Heidegger no longer analyzes existentials; he listens to language. “Language is the house of Being. In its home man dwells.” Being is not a ground we master but an Ereignis — an event of appropriation that grants epochs of intelligibility (Greek physis, Roman actualitas, modern Gestell enframing). Technology, for instance, reveals all beings as “standing-reserve” (Bestand) to be ordered and used.

The task of thinking becomes not explanation but Gelassenheit — releasement, letting beings be. This later work deeply influenced ecology, architecture, poetry, and postmodern philosophy, even as it retains the ontological difference first sharpened in 1927.

10. GNU Octave Models (for teaching simulations)

Heidegger resisted quantification, yet formal sketches can help students see structures. These four scripts run offline in GNU Octave and visualize core concepts. Paste each into a .m file.

1) temporality_model.m — Ecstatic unity

% Model Heideggerian temporality as weighted integration
% Future leads in authentic mode, present inauthentic

t = 0:0.1:20;             % lifespan proxy
future = exp(-(20-t)/5);  % coming-toward: rises near end
past = exp(-t/7);         % having-been: weight of thrownness
present = exp(-(t-10).^2/20); % making-present: peaks mid-life

authentic = 0.6*future + 0.3*past + 0.1*present;
everyday  = 0.15*future + 0.25*past + 0.6*present;

plot(t, [future; past; present; authentic; everyday]);
legend('Future','Past','Present','Authentic weighting','Everyday weighting');
title('Ecstatic Temporality: future does not follow past');
xlabel('existential time'); ylabel('disclosive weight');

2) thrownness_projection.m — Care structure

% Dasein as thrown projection: random throw + directed projection
% Ahead-of-itself + already-in

rng(1);
steps = 120;
thrown = cumsum(0.3*randn(steps,1));     % Geworfenheit: contingent drift
projection = 0.08*(1:steps)' + 0.5*sin((1:steps)'/10); % Entwurf: owned direction

dasein_path = thrown + projection;

plot(dasein_path,'k','LineWidth',1.5); hold on;
plot(thrown,'--'); plot(projection,'--');
legend('Dasein (care)','Thrownness','Projection');
title('Sorge = ahead-of-itself-Being-already-in');
ylabel('existential position');

3) ready_to_hand_breakdown.m — Smooth coping vs breakdown

% Smooth use vs breakdown reveals present-at-hand
t = linspace(0,4*pi,400);
use = sin(t);                 % transparent coping
breakdown = use;
breakdown(180:220) = NaN;     % hammer breaks: conspicuous
breakdown(181:219) = 0;       % staring at object

plot(t,use,'LineWidth',1.2); hold on;
plot(t,breakdown,'r','LineWidth',2);
title('Zuhandenheit withdraws; breakdown makes Vorhandenheit stare back');
legend('ready-to-hand flow','present-at-hand after break');
xlabel('praxis time');

4) being_toward_death.m — Authenticity increases as horizon closes

% Authenticity as function of finite horizon
horizon = 80;
age = 0:horizon;
% Everyday flees death: low authenticity until late
inauthentic = 0.2 + 0.1*sin(age/5);
% Anticipatory resoluteness: rises as death approaches
authentic = 1 - exp(-(horizon-age)/12);

plot(age,[inauthentic; authentic],'LineWidth',1.5);
legend('Inauthentic (das Man)','Authentic (anticipatory)');
title('Death as ownmost possibility re-weights the future');
xlabel('age'); ylabel('ownedness');

11. Influence, Critique, and Why He Still Matters

Heidegger’s impact is disproportionate. Jean-Paul Sartre took Dasein’s “existence precedes essence” and radicalized it into atheistic freedom in Being and Nothingness (1943), dropping Heidegger’s prioritization of Being. Maurice Merleau-Ponty extended being-in-the-world into the lived body. Hannah Arendt, Heidegger’s former student and lover, transformed thrownness into natality — the capacity to begin anew in public action.

Emmanuel Levinas accused Heidegger of subordinating ethics to ontology: the face of the Other, for Levinas, commands before Being is understood. Jacques Derrida, while deeply indebted, deconstructed Heidegger’s “metaphysics of presence” even within his own privileging of authentic temporality. Gadamer developed Heidegger’s hermeneutics into philosophical dialogue.

In analytic philosophy, Hubert Dreyfus famously used ready-to-hand to argue against symbolic AI: intelligence is not rule-following on present-at-hand representations but skillful coping. Contemporary cognitive science, embodied cognition, and enactivism echo Heidegger without always naming him.

The Political Question

No responsible reading can ignore 1933. Critics like Jürgen Habermas argue that Heidegger’s critique of modernity, his language of rootedness, destiny, and resolute community, resonates with his rectoral politics. Defenders point to his swift disillusionment and insist the philosophy contains resources to critique totalitarianism — especially the analysis of das Man and inauthentic historical destiny.

The Black Notebooks (1931–1941) complicate this defense. They contain passages linking “world Judaism” to calculative rationality, suggesting antisemitism was not merely opportunistic. Readers today must hold two tasks together: learn the ontological difference, ecstatic temporality, and the hammer, and refuse any sanctification of the thinker.

“Only a god can save us,” Heidegger told Der Spiegel in 1966, speaking of technology’s enframing. He added: “Thinking is not inactivity, but itself is a deed.”

For students, the deed is to return to the question that opens Being and Time and that never closes: what does it mean to be? Not what things exist, but how is there an open space in which you can ask, use a hammer, feel anxiety, project a life, and die your own death? Heidegger’s answer — that we are temporal clearings who care — remains unsettling because it makes philosophy not a theory but a way of existing. To read him authentically is not to repeat his words but to enact the questioning in our own historical thrownness, against the pull of what “one” simply does.
