1. Introduction: Physics as the Study of Nature

Aristotle of Stagira (384–322 BC) spent twenty years in Plato’s Academy, then tutored Alexander the Great, and finally founded his own school, the Lyceum, in Athens. Unlike his teacher Plato, who sought truth in eternal Forms beyond the senses, Aristotle insisted that knowledge begins in experience: "All men by nature desire to know," he opens the Metaphysics, and they first know by seeing, touching, and remembering.

His eight books of the Physikē Akroasis — literally “lectures on nature” — are not “physics” in our narrow modern sense. For Aristotle, physis covers everything that has an inner source of change: stones falling, seeds sprouting, animals desiring, cities growing. It includes biology, psychology, astronomy, and what we would call metaphysics.

Method matters. Aristotle does not run controlled experiments with stopwatches (those tools did not exist). Instead he practices careful observation, collects endoxa (reputable opinions), draws distinctions, and reasons from principles. His science is qualitative, teleological, and grounded in common sense. That approach dominated Western thought for nearly 2,000 years — not because people were foolish, but because it explained ordinary experience brilliantly.

“For we do not think that we know a thing until we are acquainted with its primary causes or first principles, and have carried our analysis as far as its elements.” — Physics I.1

Against Plato: Plato said the true bed is the Form of Bed, this wooden one is only a shadow. Aristotle reverses it: the form is in the wood, not above it. You cannot separate matter and form in reality, only in thought. That single move makes nature intelligible from within, not by escape to another world.

For a 21st-century reader, Aristotle feels both alien and familiar. Alien in his final causes and geocentrism. Familiar in his insistence that science answers “why,” not just “how,” and that explanation must respect what things are trying to become.

2. Core Metaphysical Foundations

Hylomorphism: Matter and Form

Every natural substance is a compound of hyle (matter) and morphe (form). Matter is the potential to be something; form is what makes it actually that thing. A bronze statue: bronze is matter, the shape of Hermes is form. Take away the form, you have a lump; take away the matter, you have only an idea. Nature works by form informing matter.

Potentiality and Actuality

Aristotle’s master distinction is between dynamis (potentiality, capacity) and entelecheia (actuality, fulfillment). Change is not a mystery if you see it as the passage from potential to actual.

Substance

A substance (ousia) is a “this-something” that persists through change — Socrates, this horse, that oak. Its accidents (tall, pale, sitting) can change while the substance remains. Physics studies substances insofar as they move and change. This focus on concrete individuals, rather than abstract quantities, is why Aristotelian physics is so different from ours.

3. The Four Causes: Why Things Are

Aristotle claims in Physics II.3 that we have scientific knowledge only when we grasp the aitia — the “becauses.” He identifies four, famously summarized by the scholastics as material, formal, efficient, and final.

“We do not have knowledge of a thing until we have grasped its why, that is to say, its cause.”

Four Causes Builder

Why four? Because “why” questions are ambiguous. Why is this statue here? Because it’s bronze (material), because it looks like Pericles (formal), because a sculptor hammered it (efficient), and because we wanted to honor the city (final). Modern science largely abandoned formal and final causes after the 17th century, keeping only material and efficient. Aristotle would say we then lose the most explanatory causes — the purpose and the what-it-is.

4. Physics Proper: Motion, Place, and the Cosmos

What is Motion?

Aristotle’s definition in Physics III.1 is famously dense: motion (kinesis) is “the actuality of what is potentially, insofar as it is potential.” Take building: bricks are potentially a house. While being built, their potential is being made actual — that in-between state is motion. Once built, motion stops. Motion is therefore incomplete actuality.

Four Kinds of Change

  1. Substantial — generation and corruption (a man comes to be, dies)
  2. Qualitative — alteration (green to red, hot to cold)
  3. Quantitative — growth and diminution
  4. Local — change of place, phora, which is primary because it underlies the others

Natural Place and the Five Elements

All sublunary bodies are mixtures of four elements, each with a natural motion toward its natural place in the cosmos. Earth (cold+dry) moves down to center; Water (cold+wet) above earth; Air (hot+wet) above water; Fire (hot+dry) up to the lunar sphere. Beyond the moon, a fifth element, aether, moves naturally in perfect circles, unchanging.

Aristotelian Cosmos

Click a sphere. Aristotle’s universe is finite, spherical, geocentric. There is no void beyond the outermost sphere of fixed stars. Motion is explained by nature and by the Unmoved Mover, who causes eternal circular motion by being loved.

Natural vs Violent Motion

Natural motion requires no continuing cause: a stone falls because it is earthy and seeks the center. Violent (forced) motion — throwing a stone up — requires a continuous mover in contact. But what pushes a projectile after it leaves the hand? Aristotle’s answer: antiperistasis — the air in front is pushed aside, rushes around behind, and pushes the projectile forward until the impulse decays.

Aristotle’s Law of Motion

In Physics IV.8 and VII.5, speed v is proportional to motive force (weight W for falling bodies) and inversely proportional to resistance of medium (density ρ): v ∝ W / ρ. Hence heavier bodies fall faster, and in a vacuum (ρ=0), speed would be infinite — impossible. Therefore, a vacuum cannot exist. Nature abhors a void.

Time, Place, Infinity

Place is the inner boundary of the containing body. Time is “the number of motion with respect to before and after” — it requires a soul to count. There is no actual infinite body or actual infinite void, only potential infinity: you can always divide a continuum or add numbers, but never complete an infinite magnitude. This blocks Zeno’s paradoxes: the continuum is potentially, not actually, infinitely divided.

6. GNU Octave Examples

Aristotle’s physics can be expressed mathematically to see its structure. Copy these scripts into Octave to experiment.

% aristotelian_fall.m
% Implements v = k * W / rho (Physics VII.5)
clear; clc;

k = 0.5;           % proportionality constant (medium-dependent)
W = [5, 10];       % weights of two stones
rho = 1.2;         % air density
t = 0:0.05:10;     % time

for i = 1:2
  v = k * W(i) / rho;   % Aristotle: constant terminal speed
  y = v * t;
  plot(t, y, 'LineWidth', 2); hold on;
end
hold off; grid on;
xlabel('time (s)'); ylabel('distance fallen');
legend('W=5','W=10','Location','northwest');
title('Aristotelian Fall: heavier falls proportionally faster');
% compare_aristotle_galileo.m
clear; clc;

W = 5; rho = 1.2; k = 0.5;
g = 9.81;
t = 0:0.02:3;

v_arist = k * W / rho * ones(size(t));
y_arist = v_arist .* t;

v_gal = g * t;
y_gal = 0.5 * g * t.^2;

subplot(2,1,1); plot(t,v_arist,'-',t,v_gal,'--','LineWidth',1.5);
grid on; legend('Aristotle (constant)','Galileo (v=gt)'); ylabel('velocity');

subplot(2,1,2); plot(t,y_arist,'-',t,y_gal,'--','LineWidth',1.5);
grid on; legend('Aristotle','Galileo'); xlabel('time'); ylabel('distance');
% natural_place.m
% Computes equilibrium height based on elemental composition
% Earth=0, Water=0.25, Air=0.5, Fire=1.0
clear;

comp = [0.7 0.2 0.1 0]; % [earth water air fire] fractions sum to 1
levels = [0 0.25 0.5 1.0];

h_natural = sum(comp .* levels);
fprintf('Natural place height (0=center, 1=moon): %.3f\n', h_natural);

% heavier (more earth) -> lower; lighter (more fire) -> higher
% projectile_antiperistasis.m
% Simple iterative model of Aristotle's air-push theory
clear; dt = 0.02; t = 0:dt:4;
x = zeros(size(t)); y = zeros(size(t));
vx = 20*cosd(30); vy = 20*sind(30);
push = 5;  % initial air impulse

for i=2:length(t)
  drag = 0.15;
  vx = vx*(1-drag*dt) + push*dt;
  vy = vy*(1-drag*dt) + push*dt*0.2 - 1.5*dt; % slight natural downward tendency
  push = push * 0.92; % air support decays
  x(i)=x(i-1)+vx*dt; y(i)=y(i-1)+vy*dt;
  if y(i)<0, break; end
end
plot(x(x>0), y(y>0), 'LineWidth',2); grid on;
xlabel('range'); ylabel('height');
title('Projectile under antiperistasis (air pushes from behind)');

Newton did not refute Aristotle by pure logic; he changed the question. Instead of “what is a stone trying to do?” he asked “how can we predict its position mathematically?” That shift from natures to laws made modern science possible — but it also left purpose to biology and ethics.

8. Legacy and Critique

John Philoponus (6th c.) in Alexandria first challenged Aristotle empirically, dropping weights and observing they fell at nearly the same rate. He proposed an impressed impetus rather than air-push.

Avicenna and Averroes preserved and refined Aristotle in the Islamic world, influencing medieval Europe. Avicenna distinguished forced and natural inclinations.

Jean Buridan (14th c.) developed impetus theory: the thrower imparts a quality that gradually corrupts, no need for surrounding air. This directly foreshadows inertia.

Galileo (1632) used inclined planes, measured times, and showed distance ∝ t², contradicting v∝W. He also argued for the conceptual possibility of vacuum.

Newton replaced natural place with universal gravitation. Heavy and light fall identically because mass cancels in F=ma.

Yet Aristotle remains vital. His four causes structure contemporary biology (material=DNA, formal=morphology, efficient=development, final=function). His virtue ethics dominates moral philosophy. And his insistence that science explain why, not just calculate how, still challenges reductionism.

9. Study Questions

  2. Recast a modern technology (e.g., smartphone) in terms of the four causes. Which cause is hardest to capture physically?
