Orientation

One tradition, two temperaments

Augustine of Hippo (354–430) and Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274) are the two load-bearing columns of Western Christian thought — and almost perfect opposites in temperament. Augustine writes in the first person, at night, out of crisis: a stolen pear, a burning city, a heart that cannot rest. Aquinas writes in no person at all, in the serene third-person machinery of the disputed question, as though truth were a cathedral and he its master builder. Reading them together is the point: Aquinas cites Augustine more than any authority except Scripture, and half the Summa is Augustine's fire rebuilt in stone.

Augustine

354–430 · Thagaste, Carthage, Milan, Hippo · rhetor, bishop
  • Converted Platonist: truth found by turning inward, then upward
  • Genres of crisis — confession, polemic, sermon, letter
  • Master themes: will, grace, sin, memory, time, love
  • Evil explained as privation; the divided will described from inside
  • History as the drama of two loves, two cities

Aquinas

1225–1274 · Roccasecca, Naples, Paris, Cologne · Dominican friar, professor
  • Baptized Aristotelian: truth found by ascending from the world
  • Genre of order — the quaestio, thousands of them, one architecture
  • Master themes: being, essence, analogy, virtue, law, grace
  • Faith and reason as two harmonious gifts of the same God
  • Grace does not destroy nature but perfects it

The three books divide the labor. The Confessions (c. 397–400) is the soul's story — the first great autobiography, addressed entirely to God with the reader overhearing. The City of God (413–426) is history's story, written in the smoke of the sack of Rome: a theology of politics, empire, and the meaning of the whole human timeline. The Summa Theologiae (1266–1273) is everything's story — God, creation, human acts, virtue, law, Christ — told as a single ordered curriculum for beginners, and left unfinished when its author, three months before his death, declared everything he had written to be straw.

The Lives

A convert and a friar, 850 years apart

  • 354Augustine born at Thagaste in Roman North Africa (Souk Ahras, Algeria) — provincial, ambitious, brilliant; his mother Monica a Christian, his father Patricius a pagan.
  • 371–383Student and then teacher of rhetoric at Carthage — "a cauldron of illicit loves." Takes a concubine (fifteen faithful years; their son Adeodatus). Reads Cicero's lost Hortensius at nineteen and falls in love with philosophy; joins the Manichees, who promise a rational religion and explain evil as an invading substance.
  • 384–386Professor of rhetoric in Milan — the empire's front row. Ambrose teaches him to read Scripture non-literally; the "books of the Platonists" teach him that evil need not be a thing. August 386: the garden, the child's voice, tolle lege.
  • 387–391Baptized by Ambrose at Easter 387. Monica dies at Ostia on the journey home. Back in Africa, his plan for a quiet philosophical retirement is wrecked when the congregation at Hippo seizes him for ordination.
  • 395–430Bishop of Hippo for thirty-five years — preacher, judge, administrator, and controversialist against Manichees, Donatists, and Pelagians. Writes the Confessions (c. 397–400) and The City of God (413–426) in the margins of a working life.
  • 410Alaric's Goths sack Rome for three days. Refugees pour into Africa asking why the Christian God failed the Christian empire — the question that begets The City of God.
  • 430Dies during the Vandal siege of Hippo, the psalms of penance pinned to his wall, as Roman Africa collapses around him.
  • — eight centuries pass: the empire falls in the West; monasteries copy; Aristotle returns through Arabic — Augustine becomes "the Theologian" of the Latin world, awaiting his greatest reader —
  • 1225Thomas born at Roccasecca castle to minor Neapolitan nobility; given as a child oblate to the great abbey of Monte Cassino — the family plan is abbot, wealth, respectability.
  • 1244–45At university in Naples he meets Aristotle's natural philosophy — and the scandalous new mendicant Dominicans. He joins the beggar-preachers; his outraged family kidnaps him and holds him in the family castle for about a year (legend: they send a prostitute to break his vow; he drives her out with a firebrand). They give up. He walks to Paris.
  • 1245–1259Studies under Albert the Great in Paris and Cologne. Classmates call the huge, silent southerner "the dumb ox"; Albert predicts the ox's bellowing will fill the world. Regent master of theology at Paris by thirty-one.
  • 1266–1273Writes the Summa Theologiae across Rome, Paris, and Naples — alongside commentaries on nearly all of Aristotle, disputed questions, and the Summa contra Gentiles — dictating, by witnesses' accounts, to several secretaries at once. In Paris he fights a two-front war: against traditionalists who fear Aristotle, and against radical Aristotelians who follow Averroes off the map.
  • 12736 December: during Mass, something happens — vision, breakdown, or both. He never writes again: "all that I have written seems to me like straw compared with what I have seen." The Summa stops mid-treatise, at penance.
  • 1274Dies 7 March at Fossanova abbey, en route to the Council of Lyon, aged about forty-nine.
  • 1277 →Paris condemnations brush some of his theses; vindication follows — canonized 1323, Doctor of the Church 1567, and in 1879 Leo XIII's Aeterni Patris makes Thomism the semi-official philosophy of the Catholic Church.
The First Book · c. 397–400

Confessions

Nothing like it had ever been written. Thirteen books addressed not to a patron or a public but to God — prayer, autobiography, philosophy, and scriptural commentary fused into a single act of confessio, a word that for Augustine means both admission of sin and praise. The reader is placed in the strangest of positions: eavesdropping on a bishop telling God what God already knows, so that we may come to know it.

Fecisti nos ad te, et inquietum est cor nostrum donec requiescat in te. "You have made us toward yourself, and our heart is restless until it rests in you." — Confessions I.1, the book's thesis, stated in its opening paragraph

The structure puzzled readers for centuries: nine books of life story, then — just when the plot resolves — four books on memory, time, and Genesis. But the shape is the argument. Books I–IX show one soul's journey from dispersion to rest; Books X–XIII widen the lens until the same journey is visible in the mind itself (X), in time itself (XI), and in creation itself (XII–XIII). The autobiography is a specimen; the metaphysics is the theory of the specimen.

Books I–VI · The long way out

Infancy, the pear tree, the cauldron of Carthage

Augustine reads his own childhood without sentimentality — the greedy infant, the schoolboy beaten for preferring play, the adolescent whose real education was ambition. The set piece is the theft of pears (Book II): sixteen years old, he and a gang strip a neighbor's pear tree, not to eat the fruit — they throw it to pigs — but for the theft itself. Augustine circles the incident obsessively, because it breaks the classical theory of action: he desired nothing, no good, however false; he loved the sin itself, and loved it because he was not alone. Evil here is not a pursued object but a defect of love, socially amplified — the seed of everything he will say against the Manichees. Carthage brings lust and the theater; Cicero's Hortensius ignites the love of wisdom at nineteen; the Manichees capture him for nine years with their promise of pure reason and their materialist solution to evil (a kingdom of darkness invading the light). Their star bishop Faustus turns out, on examination, to be a charming lightweight — the first great disillusionment (Book V). Augustine drifts to Rome, then Milan, into Ambrose's orbit, now a skeptic of the Academic kind.

Books VII–IX · Ascent and surrender

The Platonists, the garden, Ostia

Book VII is the intellectual conversion. The "books of the Platonists" (Plotinus and Porphyry, in Latin translation) give him the two ideas that unlock everything: that reality need not be corporeal, and that evil is not a substance but a privation — a corruption of good, with no independent existence, so that the Manichee dark kingdom dissolves into grammar. He attempts the Plotinian ascent to the One and touches, for an instant, that which is — then falls back, dragged by habit. Vision is not enough; the will is sick. Book VIII stages the cure. He hears of conversions harder than his; his two wills — old and new, flesh and spirit — tear him visibly apart; he famously recalls his own young prayer for chastity, "but not yet." In a Milan garden, weeping under a fig tree, he hears a child's voice chanting tolle lege — take up and read. He opens Paul at random (Romans 13:13–14), reads the first sentence his eyes fall on, and the storm simply ends. Book IX is the aftermath: baptism, the death of his mother Monica at Ostia — preceded by the vision they share at a window, ascending together in conversation past all created things to touch eternal Wisdom for one heartbeat — and the end of the story he can tell as story.

Book X · The fields of memory

Interrogating the mind that seeks God

Now bishop, Augustine turns from who he was to what he is, and the autobiography becomes phenomenology. To seek God he must pass through memoria — and he is staggered by what he finds there: the "fields and vast palaces of memory," containing not just images but skills, numbers, affections, the memory of forgetting, and — mysteriously — the idea of the happy life that all people recognize and want, though none remember acquiring it. Memory for Augustine is not a filing cabinet but the self's depth, the inner space where God, who was never absent, can be found: "you were within me, and I was outside." The book closes with the famous late confession of a love that arrived late — sero te amavi — and an unsparing audit of his present temptations, bishop or not.

Book XI · What is time?

The distension of the soul

Asked what God was doing before creation, Augustine declines the old joke (preparing hell for people who ask deep questions) and gives the serious answer: the question is broken, because time itself is a creature — there is no "before" creation. But then, what is time? He confesses the paradox every reader recognizes: if no one asks him, he knows; if he tries to explain, he does not. The past no longer exists, the future does not yet exist, and the present has no duration — yet we measure time. His resolution: what we measure is in the mind. There are, properly, three presents — the present of things past (memory), the present of things present (attention), and the present of things future (expectation) — and time as we live it is a distentio animi, a stretching-out of the soul. His example is reciting a hymn he knows — Ambrose's Deus creator omnium — as expectation drains, syllable by syllable, through the needle's eye of attention into memory. Recite it yourself:

That stretch — expectation, attention, memory held together across a span — is what Augustine thinks time, as lived, actually is. Husserl's phenomenology of internal time-consciousness begins here, fifteen centuries early.

Books XII–XIII · In the beginning

Creation, interpretation, rest

The final books read Genesis 1 — slowly, charitably, and with a startlingly modern rule of interpretation: where a text admits many true readings, we should not fight over which one the author intended, since the author's Author intended truth. The work ends where it began, with rest: the sabbath of the seventh day as the destination of the restless heart, an evening-less peace. The Confessions closes not with a conclusion but with a prayer for it.

The Second Book · 413–426

The City of God

In August 410 Alaric's Goths did the unthinkable: Rome, unconquered for eight hundred years, fell and was sacked for three days. Pagans said the abandoned old gods were taking revenge for Christianity; refugees pressed the question on Africa's most famous bishop. Augustine's answer took thirteen years and twenty-two books — and grew from a pamphlet-occasion into the first philosophy of history: an account of the whole human story as the intertwined career of two invisible commonwealths.

The plan of the twenty-two books

Books I–X demolish. First (I–V) the civic case for the gods: they never protected Rome — her disasters long predate Christ, her virtues were her own, her empire was won by a lust for domination (libido dominandi) that Augustine dissects without mercy. Here falls the most quoted political sentence of late antiquity: remove justice, and what are kingdoms but great robber bands? — pointed with the story of the captured pirate who told Alexander the Great that they were in the same trade, differing only in fleet size. Then (VI–X) the philosophical case: even the noblest paganism, the Platonists who came nearest the truth, ended in the absurdity of worshiping demons as intermediaries. Books XI–XXII construct: the origin of the two cities in the angels and in Adam (XI–XIV), their course through history from Cain and Abel to the present (XV–XVIII), and their respective ends — the last things, judgment, hell, and the city where God is all in all (XIX–XXII).

Fecerunt itaque civitates duas amores duo. "Two loves, then, have made two cities: the earthly city by love of self carried to contempt of God, the heavenly by love of God carried to contempt of self." — City of God XIV.28

Crucial and often missed: the two cities are invisible and intermingled until the last judgment. The Church is not simply the City of God, nor Rome simply the earthly city; in this age — the saeculum — the citizenships cannot be read off anyone's institutional membership. That single move disenchants all politics: no state, however Christian, is sacred, and none, however pagan, is worthless.

Book XIX: peace, justice, and the deflation of Rome

The philosophical summit. Augustine surveys the ancient candidates for the highest good, then gives his own master concept: peace, defined as tranquillitas ordinis — the tranquility of order, each thing in its place, from the ordered organs of a body to the ordered wills of a household to the ordered concord of a city. Everything seeks peace, even war (no one wages war except to obtain some peace); even a band of robbers needs peace among themselves. Then the famous demolition of Cicero: by Scipio's definition in the Republic — a people as a multitude united by agreement about right (ius) — Rome was never a commonwealth at all, since there is no true right where there is no true justice, and no justice where God is not given his due. Augustine offers a substitute definition with the moral valence removed: a people is a multitude of rational beings united by common agreement on the objects of its love. Now every polity is real — and every polity can be judged by asking one question: what does it love? Political philosophy has never quite put the question down.

Si fallor, sum

Buried in Book XI, an argument that should look familiar. Against the Academic skeptics, Augustine observes that he is completely certain that he exists, that he knows it, and that he loves this existing and knowing — and the skeptic's trump card fails against it: if I am deceived, I am (si fallor, sum). What is deceived must exist. Twelve centuries before the Meditations, the cogito is on the page — a parallel Descartes's contemporaries pointed out to him (Arnauld, admiringly, in the Fourth Objections; Descartes replied, a little stiffly, that he was glad of such a great authority but used the certainty differently: Augustine wields it against skepticism and moves on; Descartes makes it the load-bearing foundation of a whole reconstruction of knowledge).

The Third Book · 1266–1273

Summa Theologiae

The most ambitious textbook ever attempted — and it calls itself a textbook. The prologue announces, with no visible irony, that existing works confuse beginners with useless questions and disordered repetition, and that this book will treat sacred doctrine briefly and clearly, in the order the subject itself demands. What follows is 512 questions, some 2,600 articles, and roughly a million and a half words — unfinished. Its order is its deepest teaching: all things come forth from God and all things return to God, and the Summa is that circle drawn in argument.

The quaestio: thought with load-bearing objections

The Summa's atom is the article — a miniature disputation frozen on the page, descended from the live public debates of the medieval university. Its form is a machine for honesty: the strongest available case against the author's position speaks first, and every objection is guaranteed a reply. The Five Ways

The respondeo of that article contains the five most discussed paragraphs in medieval philosophy — five "ways" (viae) from public features of the world to a first reality that, as each ends, "all call God." They are a posteriori arguments — from effects back to cause — and Aquinas thought this the only route available to unaided reason, since we lack the direct grasp of God's essence an ontological argument would need (he knew Anselm's argument, and rejected it). Note what the Ways are not: they do not argue the universe had a beginning. Aquinas famously held that reason cannot prove the world began in time (Ia q.46) — the dependence in the Ways is vertical, here and now, not a relay of dominoes into the past.

    What God is not

    Immediately after proving that God exists, Aquinas issues the Summa's most under-quoted warning: since we cannot know what God is, but only what he is not, we proceed by removal — and the following questions strip away composition, body, limit, and change. Divine simplicity is the keystone: God has no parts of any kind; in God alone, essence and existence are identical — God does not have being, God is subsistent being itself (ipsum esse subsistens). This funds the doctrine of analogy (q.13): when we say God is "good" or "wise," the words apply neither univocally (as to two creatures) nor equivocally (as "bank" to river and money), but analogically — creaturely goodness is a borrowed, participated resemblance of a goodness we cannot conceive directly. Aquinas is far closer to negative theology than his rationalist reputation suggests.

    The Second Part: the human return

    The Summa's longest stretch — over three hundred questions — is not about God but about us, because we are the creature that returns to God freely, by action. It opens with the treatise on happiness: every agent acts for an end; the ultimate end cannot be wealth, honor, fame, power, or pleasure (each is eliminated in turn); complete beatitudo is nothing less than the vision of God's essence — which no finite good, and no this-worldly life, can supply. Then the machinery of the moral life: human acts, the passions (treated with a psychologist's patience — love, hatred, hope, despair, fear, anger), and habitus — stable dispositions, of which virtues are the good kind. Aquinas weaves the four classical cardinal virtues (prudence, justice, fortitude, temperance) together with the three theological virtues (faith, hope, charity) that are not acquired but infused, and crowns the structure with charity as the form of all the virtues. The Secunda Secundae then walks through them one by one, vice by opposing vice — the most complete map of moral character ever drawn.

    The treatise on law

    Inside the Second Part sits the treatise (Ia-IIae qq.90–108) that founded natural-law theory. Law is an ordinance of reason, for the common good, made by the one who has care of the community, and promulgated. It comes in four registers: eternal law — God's governing reason for the whole creation; natural law — the rational creature's participation in the eternal law, whose first precept every human grasps without instruction: good is to be done and pursued, and evil avoided; human law — positive enactments derived from natural law (and, Aquinas says, an unjust law is not law simply speaking but a corruption of law — a sentence with a long afterlife, from resistance theory to Martin Luther King's Letter from Birmingham Jail, which cites Aquinas by name); and divine law — revealed law, given because our destiny outruns our nature's reach.

    Grace perfecting nature

    The Second Part ends where Augustine spent his last twenty years: grace. Aquinas's formula reconciles the saint's fire with the philosopher's confidence in nature — gratia non tollit naturam, sed perficit: grace does not destroy nature but perfects it. Reason, virtue, political life, friendship are real goods with real integrity; grace does not bulldoze them but heals and elevates them toward an end they could never reach alone. The whole Thomist settlement — faith and reason, church and philosophy, Aristotle and the Gospel — is compressed into those five Latin words.

    The Third Part, and the silence

    Having traced the circle's outbound and inbound arcs, the Tertia Pars supplies the road: Christ — the fittingness of the Incarnation, the events of his life treated one by one (a medieval rarity), and the sacraments as the instruments through which his healing reaches bodies as well as souls. Aquinas was in the middle of the sacrament of penance, at question 90, when the Mass of December 6, 1273 ended his writing life. Urged by his companion Reginald to continue, he answered that he could not: after what he had seen, everything he had written seemed to him like straw. The system's unfinished state is, accidentally or not, its most Thomist feature — the whole apparatus of the Summa insists that our words about God are analogical pointers beyond themselves, and its author's last act was to point.

    Across the Centuries

    The 850-year dialogue

    Open the Summa almost anywhere and Augustine is speaking — cited over a thousand times, more than any authority except Scripture, usually in the objections and in the answers. Aquinas's habitual method with his master is not refutation but charitable re-housing: the Augustinian insight is preserved, the Platonist scaffolding replaced with Aristotelian steel. The load-bearing agreements and quiet renovations:

    Evil
    Augustine: against the Manichees — evil is no substance but a privation of good; a wholly evil thing could not exist, since existence itself is good.
    Aquinas: total agreement, formalized: evil is a privation of a due good, parasitic on being; it has deficient causes, not efficient ones. The first objection to God's existence (q.2, obj.1) is answered with Augustine's own words.
    Knowledge & illumination
    Augustine: the mind knows unchangeable truths by an interior divine illumination — truth is found within, above the mind.
    Aquinas: the gentlest renovation. The "divine light" is real — but it is the created agent intellect God gives each of us; knowledge begins in the senses and rises by abstraction. Nothing in the intellect that was not first in the senses: Aristotle's empiricism, baptized.
    Grace & nature
    Augustine: after Pelagius — fallen humanity cannot will its way to God; grace is sheer gift, preceding every merit; even the beginning of faith is given.
    Aquinas: the anti-Pelagian core intact (no one merits first grace), set in a calmer frame: nature is wounded, not destroyed; grace presupposes, heals, and perfects it. The Reformation would later read this as too calm, and reach back past Thomas to Augustine's fire.
    The soul
    Augustine: Platonist inheritance — the soul is a substance using a body; the self's true life is interior.
    Aquinas: Aristotle's hylomorphism instead — the soul is the substantial form of the body; a human is not a ghost piloting a machine but a single body-soul composite. (A separated soul survives death but is, strikingly, not the complete person — hence the doctrinal weight Aquinas puts on bodily resurrection.) Between Augustine's interiority and Aquinas's composite runs the fault line Descartes would reopen.
    Time & creation
    Augustine: time is a creature; there is no "before" creation; lived time is the soul's distension.
    Aquinas: agrees creation-with-time is the truth — but holds, against nearly everyone, that reason alone cannot prove the world began: an eternal created world is philosophically possible; we know the beginning by faith. Intellectual humility as a precision instrument.
    Politics
    Augustine: the state is a remedial order for fallen wills — at best a keeper of earthly peace, judged by what it loves, never sacred.
    Aquinas: re-enter Aristotle — humans are political by nature, not merely by fall; government would exist even in Eden; law educates toward virtue. Augustinian realism and Thomist naturalism remain the two poles of Christian political thought.

    Afterlives

    The three books have distinct posthumous careers. The Confessions became the template of Western inwardness — Petrarch carried a pocket copy up Mont Ventoux; Rousseau's Confessions steals the title while reversing the addressee (man, not God); and modern philosophy of time and memory, from Husserl to Ricoeur to Wittgenstein (who opens the Philosophical Investigations with a long quotation from Confessions I on how children learn words), keeps returning to Books X–XI. The City of God fathered the philosophy of history and haunts political theology from medieval papalism through Luther's two kingdoms to twentieth-century Augustinian realists like Reinhold Niebuhr — every critique of political religion and every warning against utopia has Book XIX somewhere in its ancestry. The Summa was condemned in parts (1277), canonized with its author (1323), armored into "Thomism" by commentators, revived by papal decree (1879), and now supplies the backbone of contemporary virtue ethics and natural-law jurisprudence; analytic philosophers mine the Five Ways, the theory of analogy, and the hylomorphic account of mind with undiminished profit. And for your shelf's continuity: when Descartes sat down by his fire in 1641, both the genre he wrote in — the meditation — and the argument he found at the bottom of doubt were Augustine's; what he discarded was precisely the Thomist confidence that the senses are where knowledge begins.

    How to read them

    The Confessions: straight through, I–IX in a few sittings, then X and XI slowly — Sarah Ruden's translation (Modern Library) is vivid and startling; Chadwick (Oxford World's Classics) is the reliable standard; Peter Brown's Augustine of Hippo is the great biography. The City of God: do not read it straight through on a first pass — take Books I, IV.4, VIII, XI–XII, XIV, XIX, and XXII; Dyson (Cambridge Texts) or Bettenson (Penguin) translate; O'Donovan and Markus (Saeculum) guide the politics. The Summa: never front to back — enter through Ia q.1 (what theology is), qq.2–13 (God), Ia-IIae qq.1–5 (happiness), qq.90–97 (law), IIa-IIae q.23 (charity), and IIIa q.1 (why Incarnation); the Benziger/English Dominican translation is free everywhere online; Brian Davies's The Thought of Thomas Aquinas, Chesterton's short St. Thomas Aquinas (still beloved by scholars, against all odds), and Josef Pieper's Guide to Thomas Aquinas are the companions to trust.

    Glossary

    The working vocabulary

    confessio
    Augustine's double-edged word: confession of sin and confession of praise at once — the speech-act the entire Confessions performs.
    privatio boni
    Evil as privation of good — not a substance or positive force but a lack, a corruption in something good insofar as it exists. Common ground of all three books.
    distentio animi
    "Distension of the soul" — Augustine's account of lived time as the mind's stretch across memory, attention, and expectation (Confessions XI).
    saeculum
    The present age between Fall and Judgment, in which the two cities are inseparably intermingled — the root of "secular," coined as a theological concept.
    libido dominandi
    The lust for domination — Augustine's diagnosis of Rome's engine, and of the earthly city's politics generally.
    tranquillitas ordinis
    Peace as the tranquility of order — each thing disposed in its place; the master concept of City of God XIX.
    quaestio / articulus
    The scholastic unit of thought: a question divided into articles, each staging objections, a counter-authority, a determination, and replies.
    sed contra
    "But against this" — the pivot of an article, citing an authority on the author's side before the argument proper begins.
    quinque viae
    The Five Ways of ST I q.2 a.3: from motion, efficient causation, contingency, degrees of perfection, and the governance of things.
    ipsum esse subsistens
    Subsistent being itself — Aquinas's deepest name for God, in whom essence and existence are identical; everything else has being by participation.
    analogia
    Analogical predication — how words like "good" and "wise" apply to God: neither univocally nor equivocally, but by proportional resemblance (ST I q.13).
    habitus
    A stable disposition of a power toward acting well or badly; virtues and vices are habitus — character as second nature.
    lex naturalis
    Natural law — the rational creature's participation in God's eternal law; first precept: good is to be done and pursued, evil avoided (Ia-IIae q.94).
    gratia perficit naturam
    "Grace perfects nature" — the Thomist settlement: grace presupposes, heals, and elevates nature rather than abolishing it.
    visio beatifica
    The beatific vision — direct knowledge of God's essence, the only end in which the restless heart's demand for complete happiness is met.
