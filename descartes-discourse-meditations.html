Orientation

Two books, one project, six parts each

The Discourse and the Meditations are the same intellectual event told twice — first as autobiography for the general public, then as a formal exercise for the learned world. Their symmetry is not an accident: each unfolds in exactly six parts, and each pivots on the same hinge, the discovery that doubt itself proves a doubter.

Descartes wrote at the moment the medieval Aristotelian consensus was collapsing. Copernicus had moved the Earth; Galileo had been condemned for saying so (1633 — an event that made Descartes suppress his own physics treatise, Le Monde); the revival of ancient skepticism by Montaigne and others had made it fashionable to doubt that anything could be known at all. Descartes's wager was radical: instead of resisting skepticism, he would weaponize it — push doubt to its absolute maximum, and see whether anything survived. Whatever did survive would be a foundation no skeptic could shake, and on it the new mathematical science of nature could be rebuilt with better-than-Aristotelian credentials.

Discourse on the Method

1637 · Leiden · French · published anonymously

An intellectual autobiography — charming, readable, deliberately modest. Written in French rather than Latin so that, as Descartes says, even those who use only their pure natural reason can judge it. It was published as a preface to three scientific essays it was meant to advertise: the Dioptrics, the Meteorology, and the Geometry.

Meditations on First Philosophy

1641 · Paris · Latin · with Objections & Replies

The rigorous version — six days of guided first-person meditation, modeled loosely on the spiritual-exercise tradition of Descartes's Jesuit schooling. You are not told the conclusions; you are led to perform the reasoning yourself. Circulated in manuscript to critics before printing, so that seven sets of objections and Descartes's replies appear in the same volume.

  • I — What can be called into doubt
  • II — The mind, better known than the body
  • III — The existence of God (the trademark argument)
  • IV — Truth, falsity, and why we err
  • V — The essence of matter; God again (ontological)
  • VI — The real distinction of mind and body; the world regained

Read the Discourse to meet the man; read the Meditations to fight the arguments. This guide takes them in that order, then turns to the objections that shaped philosophy's response ever since.

The Life

A soldier, a stove, a queen's timetable

René Descartes (1596–1650) is philosophy's great late riser — he kept the lifelong habit, granted to him as a sickly boy at the Jesuit college of La Flèche, of meditating in bed until late morning, and he credited those quiet hours for most of his ideas. Trained in law at Poitiers, he chose instead to read from what he called the great book of the world: he took unpaid service in Dutch and Bavarian armies, traveled Europe, and then settled for two decades in the Dutch Republic, moving house constantly and guarding his solitude.

  • 1596Born at La Haye en Touraine, France (the town is now called Descartes). His mother dies the following year.
  • 1606–14Educated at the Jesuit college of La Flèche — excellent mathematics, Aristotelian everything else. He leaves admiring only the certainty of mathematics.
  • 1619Winter quarters near Ulm, Germany. Shut in a stove-heated room (poêle), he conceives the project of rebuilding all knowledge single-handedly; that night, three vivid dreams convince him it is his vocation.
  • 1628–29Moves to the Dutch Republic. Drafts the unfinished Rules for the Direction of the Mind.
  • 1633Galileo condemned in Rome. Descartes suppresses Le Monde, his Copernican physics, on the eve of publication.
  • 1637Discourse on the Method published anonymously with the Dioptrics, Meteorology, and Geometry.
  • 1641Meditations on First Philosophy published in Paris with six sets of Objections and Replies (a seventh added in 1642).
  • 1643Correspondence begins with Princess Elisabeth of Bohemia, whose questions about mind–body interaction he never fully answers — and who draws from him the Passions of the Soul (1649).
  • 1644Principles of Philosophy — his system in textbook form, hoping to replace Aristotle in the schools.
  • 1650Dies of pneumonia in Stockholm, where Queen Christina had insisted on philosophy lessons at five in the morning, in a Swedish winter — a cruel schedule for a man who thought best in a warm bed.
The First Book · 1637

Discourse on the Method

Full title: Discourse on the Method of Rightly Conducting One's Reason and Seeking Truth in the Sciences. It presents itself not as a treatise but as a story — "a history, or if you prefer, a fable" — of how one mind came to distrust its education and invent a method to replace it.

Part I

Good sense is the best distributed thing in the world

The famous ironic opening: everyone believes they have enough good sense, since even the hardest to please in everything else never wish for more of it than they have. Descartes's real point is egalitarian and subversive — reason is equal in all; what differs is method, the way we conduct it. He then reviews his elite education subject by subject and finds that only mathematics offered certainty, while philosophy, cultivated by the best minds for centuries, contains nothing that is not disputed. So he abandons the study of letters for the study of the world — and finally, of himself.

Part II

The stove-heated room and the four rules

Winter 1619, Germany. Detained by weather with no company and no cares, Descartes spends the day shut in a stove-heated room, alone with his thoughts. His governing image is architectural: buildings designed by a single architect are more orderly and beautiful than cities that grew piecemeal. Likewise the sciences, assembled from the opinions of many, would be better razed and rebuilt by one mind on its own foundations. He is careful to insist this is a private renovation, not a political program — he condemns reformers who would tear down the public order. In place of the sprawling logic of the schools he adopts just four precepts, resolving never once to fail to observe them:

1 · Evidence

Accept nothing as true that is not known evidently to be so — avoid precipitation and prejudice, and include in judgments only what presents itself so clearly and distinctly to the mind that there is no occasion to doubt it.

2 · Division

Divide each difficulty into as many parts as possible and as may be required to resolve it best. (Analysis: cut the problem down to tractable pieces.)

3 · Order

Conduct thoughts in order, beginning with the simplest and easiest objects and ascending step by step to the most complex — even assuming an order among things that have no natural precedence.

4 · Enumeration

Make enumerations so complete and reviews so general as to be assured of omitting nothing. (Audit the whole chain; a proof is only as strong as its weakest recalled link.)

These are, transparently, a mathematician's habits generalized — the procedure by which Descartes had already fused algebra and geometry into a single science. The method's promise: all things knowable by humans follow from one another in the same way as the long chains of simple reasonings used by geometers.

Part III

A provisional morality — how to live while the house is down

If you demolish your house, you still need somewhere to lodge while rebuilding. So Descartes adopts a morale par provision of three maxims (plus a resolution): first, obey the laws, customs, and religion of his country, following the most moderate opinions of the most sensible people around him; second, be as firm and resolute in action as possible, following even doubtful opinions, once adopted, as if they were certain — like travelers lost in a forest who do best to walk as straight as they can in one direction rather than circle; third, seek always to conquer himself rather than fortune, to change his desires rather than the order of the world, training himself to believe that nothing is fully in our power except our thoughts — a deliberately Stoic note. Finally, he resolves to devote his life to cultivating his reason. Note the asymmetry that critics from Gassendi onward found suspicious: total demolition in theory, total conformism in practice.

Part IV

Je pense, donc je suis

The metaphysical core, compressed into a few pages (the Meditations will expand it to a book). Since the senses sometimes deceive, suppose they always do; since men err in reasoning, reject all demonstrations; since all waking thoughts can occur in dreams, suppose everything that had ever entered his mind is no more true than the illusions of his dreams. But immediately he notices: while he was trying to think everything false, it was necessary that he, who was thinking this, was something.

Je pense, donc je suis. "I think, therefore I am" — Discourse, Part IV. The Latin tag cogito, ergo sum appears in the Principles of Philosophy (1644); the Meditations, interestingly, never uses the "therefore" formula at all.

This truth is so firm that the most extravagant skeptical suppositions cannot shake it, so he accepts it as the first principle of his philosophy. From it he extracts a criterion — whatever is perceived very clearly and very distinctly is true — and then arguments for God: the idea of a perfect being in an imperfect mind must have a cause adequate to its content, and existence is contained in the idea of a perfect being as necessarily as the angle-sum is contained in the idea of a triangle. God, being perfect, is no deceiver, and this underwrites the reliability of clear and distinct perception.

Part V

The world as machine

A summary of the suppressed Le Monde: light, the heavens, the formation of a hypothetical world from matter and laws of motion alone — a fable that would, of course, end up indistinguishable from ours. He describes the circulation of the blood (crediting the English physician who demonstrated it, William Harvey, though Descartes wrongly disputes Harvey's account of the heart as a muscular pump, preferring his own theory of cardiac heat rarefying the blood). Animals, he argues, are automata — exquisitely made machines without thought. Two tests distinguish humans from any possible machine: the language test (a machine could never arrange words to give an appropriately meaningful answer to whatever is said in its presence, as the dullest human can) and the generality test (a machine acts from the disposition of its organs, a special-purpose instrument, while reason is a universal instrument). Three centuries later this passage became a founding text for debates about artificial intelligence — the Turing test is Descartes's language test with the polarity reversed.

Part VI

Masters and possessors of nature

Why publish? Galileo's condemnation taught caution; but the method has borne fruits too useful to withhold. Instead of the speculative philosophy of the schools, a practical philosophy is possible by which, knowing the powers of fire, water, air, and the heavens as distinctly as artisans know their trades, we could render ourselves as it were the masters and possessors of nature — above all for the conservation of health, the first good of this life. He asks the public for observations and funding for experiments: the modern research program, complete with a grant proposal, in 1637.

The Geometry: the method shown, not told

The Discourse's three appended essays were the method's demonstration pieces, and the third changed mathematics permanently. La Géométrie shows that every curve can be an equation and every equation a curve — algebra and geometry are one science. The coordinate plane that every student now learns is named for him: Cartesian. Trace the parabola below and watch the translation between shape and formula happen in real time — this dictionary between the visual and the symbolic is the method's rule of division and order made into a tool.

The Second Book · 1641

Meditations on First Philosophy

Six days. One meditator — deliberately anonymous, so that the "I" of the text can be you. The genre is borrowed from the Jesuit spiritual exercises of Descartes's schooling, but the purgation here is epistemic: on the first day everything is burned down to the frost line, and over five more days the world is rebuilt, warmer and stranger than before.

The first edition's subtitle promised demonstrations of God's existence and the soul's immortality; the second edition corrected the overreach — what is actually demonstrated is the distinction between the human soul and the body. Descartes told Mersenne privately that the book contained the foundations of his physics smuggled in, arranged so that readers would accept them before noticing they destroyed the principles of Aristotle.

I What can be called into doubt De iis quae in dubium revocari possunt

The scene: a man seated by the fire, in a winter dressing gown, paper in his hands, finally free of cares — and resolved, once in his life, to demolish everything and start again from the foundations. The strategy is architectural and efficient: no need to examine each belief (an endless task); attack the foundations, and whatever was built on them falls with them. And a belief need not be proven false to be condemned — for foundations, anything not completely certain and indubitable is treated as if false.

Doubt then arrives in three escalating waves. Each wave freezes a larger territory; run the demolition yourself:

The three waves of doubt
Nothing has yet been doubted. Every belief in the wall still stands in candlelight.

Note the fine structure. Wave 1 spares nearby, medium-sized objects (that these are my hands). Wave 2 freezes all particular sensory beliefs but spares the general kinds from which dreams are painted — extension, shape, number — so mathematics still stands: awake or asleep, 2 + 3 = 5. Only Wave 3 reaches arithmetic itself. And one belief no wave can touch.

Two details are often missed. First, madness is raised and set aside — the meditator will not compare himself to those who insist their heads are made of earthenware, "but they are mad"; the dream argument then does the same work politely, since a dreamer is a temporary madman. Second, the demon is not an argument but a discipline: Descartes admits his habitual opinions will keep creeping back, so he deliberately pretends they are false — a counterweight to bend the stick the other way. The Meditation ends in dread: like a prisoner enjoying an imaginary freedom in sleep, he fears waking into the inextricable darkness of the problems just raised.

II The nature of the human mind — better known than the body De natura mentis humanae: quod ipsa sit notior quam corpus

Yesterday's doubts feel like deep water — he can neither touch bottom nor swim to the surface. But Archimedes asked only one firm and immovable point to move the whole earth; one certainty, however small, is enough. And here it is: even if the demon deceives me about everything, he cannot deceive me into not existing, for something must exist to be deceived.

Ego sum, ego existo — quoties a me profertur, vel mente concipitur, necessario esse verum. "I am, I exist — is necessarily true whenever it is put forward by me or conceived in my mind." Meditation II. No "therefore" — the certainty is grasped in the performance of thinking, not deduced from a premise.

But what am I? Not, so far as I yet know, a man, a rational animal, a body — all of these are doubtable. The only attribute that cannot be peeled away is thought: I am, precisely, a thing that thinks — res cogitans — a thing that doubts, understands, affirms, denies, wills, refuses, and also imagines and senses. Note the crucial breadth: "thought" covers everything of which I am immediately conscious, including seeming-to-see. The demon may fake the light, but not the seeming.

The piece of wax

Then the most famous thought experiment in the book — aimed not at the wax but at us, at our stubborn conviction that bodies are known by the senses. Take a piece of wax fresh from the honeycomb and bring it toward the fire. Every sensory quality changes; the wax remains. So what were we knowing, when we knew the wax?

Every sensory quality is intact — and yet none of them is the wax, as the fire is about to prove.

The moral: the wax is grasped as something extended, flexible, and movable — and since it is capable of innumerable changes of shape that imagination could never run through, this grasp is not imagination either. Bodies are perceived not by the senses or imagination but by the intellect alone (solius mentis inspectio). Even the everyday judgment "I see men crossing the square" outruns sense — from the window one sees only hats and coats, which could conceal automata; that they are men is a judgment. And the twist of the title: every perception of the wax, even a mistaken one, proves the perceiver more evidently than the wax. The mind is better known than any body.

III The existence of God De Deo, quod existat

Eyes shut, ears stopped, senses withdrawn, the meditator takes inventory. From the cogito he extracts a general rule: whatever I perceive very clearly and very distinctly is true — for that is exactly what certified "I am." But the demon hypothesis still hangs over even arithmetic when I am not attending to the proof. To secure knowledge as a stable possession rather than a flickering present-tense certainty, he must ask whether there is a God, and whether he can be a deceiver.

The machinery: ideas sorted by origin — innate (thought, truth), adventitious (apparently from outside: heat, sound), and invented (sirens, hippogriffs). Then a scholastic distinction with a starring role: an idea has formal reality as a mental event (all ideas equal here) but also objective reality — the reality of what it represents. And a causal principle taken as self-evident: there must be at least as much reality in the total cause as in the effect — applied even to the objective reality of ideas. Something cannot come from nothing, nor the more perfect from the less perfect.

The trademark argument (causal argument from the idea of God)
  1. I find in myself an idea of God: an infinite, independent, supremely intelligent, supremely powerful substance, creator of everything else.
  2. Every idea's objective reality requires a cause with at least as much formal reality.
  3. I am a finite substance; the objective reality of the idea of an infinite substance exceeds anything I could supply. (Nor is the infinite a mere negation of the finite — I grasp my finitude against a prior grasp of the infinite.)
  4. The idea could not be assembled by amplifying my ideas of finite perfections; a potentially ever-increasing perfection is precisely not actual infinity.
  5. The idea of God was placed in me by God — like the mark of the craftsman stamped on his work. Therefore God exists.

A second causal argument follows: could I, who have this idea, exist if there were no God? Not self-caused (I would have given myself every perfection), not sustained by myself (conservation requires the same power as creation, and I feel no such power), and a regress of finite causes must terminate in a being that has the idea of God and all the perfections it represents. The Meditation closes on an un-argumentative note: a pause to gaze on the divine immensity — the one openly devotional moment in the book, and a reminder of the meditation genre it inhabits. Since deception is a defect and God possesses all perfections, God is no deceiver — the warm principle from which the rest of the world will be rebuilt.

IV Truth and falsity De vero et falso

A new problem, self-inflicted: if a non-deceiving God made me, why do I ever err? The answer is Descartes's theodicy of mistake, and it turns on an asymmetry inside the mind. My intellect — the faculty that perceives ideas — is finite; there is no defect in its being limited, only in pretending otherwise. My will — the faculty of affirming and denying — is, in its kind, effectively unbounded: I can affirm or withhold about anything whatsoever; it is above all in virtue of the will that I bear the image and likeness of God.

Error occurs when the infinite will outruns the finite intellect — when I affirm what I do not clearly and distinctly perceive. God is not the author of my errors; I am, by misusing a faculty that is good. And the remedy is a discipline, not an upgrade: withhold assent from everything not clearly and distinctly perceived. Judgment, for Descartes, is an act of will — a striking doctrine (Spinoza would later attack it head-on, collapsing will into intellect) that makes believing something we do, and can therefore do responsibly or recklessly. Meditation IV thus doubles as the book's ethics of belief: the four rules of the Discourse, refounded in the anatomy of the soul.

V The essence of material things; God again De essentia rerum materialium; & iterum de Deo, quod existat

Before asking whether bodies exist, ask what they would be. What I clearly and distinctly perceive in body is exhaustively geometric: continuous quantity — extension in length, breadth, and depth — with size, shape, position, motion, duration. This is the metaphysical charter for mathematical physics: matter just is what geometry is about. These essences are discovered, not invented — a triangle has properties I must prove and cannot legislate, whether or not any triangle exists outside my thought.

And one essence contains a surprise. In every other case, essence and existence are separable — I can conceive the triangle without conceiving any existing triangle. But the idea of a supremely perfect being contains existence the way the triangle contains its angle-sum, or a mountain contains a valley:

The ontological argument, Cartesian version
  1. I have a clear and distinct idea of God as the supremely perfect being.
  2. Whatever I clearly and distinctly perceive to belong to the nature of a thing can be truly affirmed of it.
  3. Necessary existence is a perfection, and I clearly and distinctly perceive that it belongs to the nature of a supremely perfect being — to conceive God without existence is to conceive a mountain without a valley.
  4. Therefore God exists — the one being whose essence guarantees existence.

Descartes anticipates the classic objection (thinking doesn't make it so — I can imagine a winged horse): the necessity runs from the thing to the thought, not the reverse. I am not free to conceive God without existence, as I am free to imagine a horse with or without wings. Kant's later reply — that existence is not a real predicate — was aimed squarely at this argument. The Meditation ends by cashing the epistemic check: with a non-deceiving God secured, clear and distinct perceptions can be trusted even when I am no longer attending to the proofs. An atheist geometer can be certain while the demonstration is before his mind; only the theist's knowledge survives looking away — becomes scientia, stable science, rather than flickering conviction.

VI The real distinction; the world regained De rerum materialium existentia, & reali mentis a corpore distinctione

The longest meditation, and the one doing the most jobs: distinguish intellect from imagination, prove mind and body are distinct substances, prove bodies exist after all, explain the mind–body union, and finally dismiss the dream doubt that started everything.

Imagination is not intellect: the chiliagon

Understanding a triangle, I also picture it. Understanding a chiliagon — a thousand-sided polygon — I picture, at best, a vague blur indistinguishable from the blur for a 10,000-gon; yet my understanding of the two figures is perfectly distinct (I can prove different theorems about each). Imagination is therefore a limited, effortful, body-involving faculty, inessential to the mind. Try it:

Somewhere past a few dozen sides, the picture stops keeping up — the polygon becomes visually a circle — while the concept stays exact (the interior angle of a chiliagon is 179.64°, and you know that without seeing it). That gap between the blur and the theorem is the gap between imagination and pure intellect.

The real distinction of mind and body

The argument for dualism
  1. Whatever I clearly and distinctly conceive can be created by God exactly as I conceive it; so if I can clearly and distinctly conceive two things apart, they are really distinct (separable, at least by God's power).
  2. I have a clear and distinct idea of myself as a thinking, non-extended thing.
  3. I have a clear and distinct idea of body as an extended, non-thinking thing.
  4. Therefore I (the mind) am really distinct from my body, and can exist without it.

Everything hangs on the premise that the conceived separation is complete — that "thinking thing" is my whole essence and not just the part of myself I happen to have noticed (Arnauld's objection, below). Note also what the argument delivers and what it doesn't: distinctness and separability, hence the possibility of survival after death — not immortality, which Descartes admitted would need further premises about God's will.

Bodies exist — and the union is not a pilot in a ship

The proof that the external world exists is almost anticlimactic: I have a passive faculty of sensing, which would be useless without an active cause of my sensations; that cause is not me (sensations arrive unbidden); God has given me a great propensity to believe they come from bodies, and no faculty to correct it; a non-deceiving God would not arrange that. Therefore bodies exist — though they may be nothing like the senses paint them; what is guaranteed is only what is clear and distinct in them, namely the geometric. Sensory qualities are demoted to signals: teachings of nature for the preservation of the composite, not for physics. Thirst teaches me to drink, not what water is.

And here the book's deepest tension surfaces, stated by Descartes himself: I am not lodged in my body as a pilot in a ship — I am most tightly joined and as it were intermingled with it, so that body and I compose one thing. A pilot perceives damage to the hull by inspection; I feel damage to my body as pain. The dualist thus owes an account of a union his own dualism makes mysterious — the debt Princess Elisabeth would call in two years later.

Waking from the dream

Last, the dream argument is retired — not refuted, but outgrown. Waking life is coherent: memory connects present experiences to the whole rest of life, as dreams never are; a man who appeared and vanished like a specter would rightly be judged a phantom. With all my faculties checked against each other and a non-deceiving God behind them, the hyperbolic doubts of the past days deserve to be dismissed as laughable. The book ends on a candid human note: the press of practical life means we must often act before certainty is available — the life of man is subject to error, and we must acknowledge the weakness of our nature.

The Built-In Critics

Objections & Replies

Descartes did something almost without precedent: he circulated the Meditations in manuscript, through his friend Marin Mersenne, to the sharpest minds available — and published their attacks, with his replies, inside the book itself. Seven sets in all, and roughly half the volume. The greatest hits:

CaterusFirst Objections · Dutch theologian
Presses the scholastic credentials of "objective reality" and revives Aquinas's complaint against ontological arguments: the argument shows only that the concept of God includes existence — that "if God exists, God exists necessarily." Descartes: necessary existence in the idea of God is unique to that idea; the inference from true idea to reality holds only there.
HobbesThird Objections · materialist
Grants "I think, therefore I am" but denies the leap to an immaterial thinker: why not conclude that the thinking thing is corporeal? Reasoning, says Hobbes, may just be the motion of matter; and we have no idea of God at all, only a name. Descartes: visibly irritated, gives curt replies — the exchange is a near-total standoff between the two frameworks, and reads today like the first round of the materialism debate that never ended.
ArnauldFourth Objections · theologian, the sharpest reader
Two immortal objections. (1) From clearly conceiving myself without body it doesn't follow that body doesn't belong to my essence — my conception may be incomplete, like someone who clearly grasps a right triangle while ignorant of the Pythagorean relation. (2) The Cartesian Circle: we are sure clear and distinct perceptions are true only because God exists — but sure God exists only through clear and distinct perceptions. Descartes: to (1), the ideas of mind and body are each complete ideas; to (2), see the widget below.
GassendiFifth Objections · Epicurean priest
Addresses Descartes mockingly as "O Mind" (Descartes retaliates with "O Flesh"). The longest and most empiricist set: the wax's "extension" is itself known through the senses; "I walk, therefore I am" would do as well as the cogito; and the idea of infinity is manufactured by amplifying finite ideas. Descartes: "I walk" fails — walking can be doubted (dreams), seeming-to-walk cannot; and amplification never yields the actual infinite.
Mersenne & companySecond & Sixth Objections · compiled
Raised the circle before Arnauld, asked how an atheist geometer can know anything, and — momentously — requested that Descartes recast his arguments in geometrical form. Descartes: appends to the Second Replies a synthetic exposition more geometrico — definitions, axioms, propositions. Spinoza read it closely; his first published book (1663) was a geometrical exposition of Descartes, and the Ethics is that request taken to its limit.
BourdinSeventh Objections · Jesuit, added 1642
A sprawling, sarcastic attack on the method of doubt itself — if you renounce everything doubtful, you may never get anything back. Descartes: replies with equal sarcasm; the exchange mostly shows how badly he wanted, and failed, to win over the Jesuits who had educated him.

The Cartesian Circle, up close

The most durable objection deserves its own diagram. Does the system validate clear and distinct perception by God, and God by clear and distinct perception? Toggle between the charge and the standard reconstruction of Descartes's defense:

Arnauld's circleThe charge

Descartes's reply (Second and Fourth Replies) distinguishes present, attended clear-and-distinct perception — which the demon cannot touch while it lasts, and which never needed a divine guarantee — from the standing reliability of conclusions I merely remember having demonstrated. God secures the second, not the first. Whether this "memory defense" (or the alternative readings scholars have proposed) actually dissolves the circle remains one of the great open questions of Descartes scholarship — many hold that the doubt Descartes raises about reason is one that no argument, by construction, could answer.

Aftermath

What the fire left behind

Elisabeth's question

In May 1643, Princess Elisabeth of Bohemia asked the question the Meditations had left glowing: how can an immaterial, unextended soul move the body, when everything we understand about moving things involves contact and extension? Descartes's answers — that mind–body union is a third "primitive notion" alongside thought and extension, best understood not by metaphysics but by everyday life, and that the soul acts on the body at the pineal gland — satisfied neither Elisabeth nor posterity. He even conceded to her, remarkably, that it is easiest to grasp the union by not philosophizing. The interaction problem became early modern philosophy's engine: occasionalism (Malebranche), pre-established harmony (Leibniz), and dual-aspect monism (Spinoza) are all attempted repairs of the Cartesian house.

Three centuries of return fire

Nearly every major philosopher since has defined a position against some Cartesian thesis. Locke and the empiricists denied innate ideas. Hume reported that introspection finds only a bundle of perceptions, never the substantial self the cogito was thought to deliver. Kant argued that the "I think" is a formal condition of experience, not an object of knowledge, and demolished the ontological argument. In the twentieth century, Ryle mocked substance dualism as the dogma of "the ghost in the machine"; Wittgenstein's private-language considerations attacked the idea that inner experience could be an epistemically private foundation; Lichtenberg's old quip — the evidence licenses only "it thinks," not "I think" — became a standard worry about how much the cogito really proves.

Yet the arguments refuse to die on schedule. Kripke — in Naming and Necessity, arguing with rigid designators rather than divine separability — contended that the identity theorist cannot brush aside the Cartesian modal intuition: if "pain" and "C-fiber stimulation" both designate rigidly, and their identity is conceivably false, the identity theory has a problem that the usual analogies (heat and molecular motion) cannot dissolve. Meditation VI's argument, in modern dress, is still on the table. And the Discourse's language test for machine minds reads, in the age of conversational AI, less like a historical curiosity than an open experimental question.

How to read them

Practical counsel for a first serious pass. Read the Discourse in one or two sittings — it is short and was built for that. Read the Meditations as its genre demands: one meditation at a time, ideally on separate days, doing the doubting rather than watching it. Then read Objections IV (Arnauld) and Descartes's replies before forming a verdict on the circle and the real distinction — Descartes at his best is Descartes under fire. The standard English edition is Cottingham, Stoothoff, and Murdoch, The Philosophical Writings of Descartes (Cambridge, 3 vols.; the correspondence, including Elisabeth, is in vol. III). Strong companions: Bernard Williams, Descartes: The Project of Pure Enquiry; Margaret Wilson, Descartes; and the Cambridge Companion to Descartes' Meditations.

Glossary

The working vocabulary

cogito
Shorthand for the first certainty. Je pense, donc je suis (Discourse); ego sum, ego existo (Meditations); cogito, ergo sum (Principles). Whether it is an inference, an intuition, or a performance is a classic dispute.
res cogitans / res extensa
Thinking substance and extended substance — the two created kinds of thing. Mind's whole essence is thought; body's whole essence is extension.
clear and distinct
The twin marks of trustworthy perception. Clear: present and accessible to the attentive mind (as objects are clear to a well-focused eye). Distinct: so sharply separated from all other perceptions that it contains nothing but what is clear.
hyperbolic doubt
Doubt pushed beyond all practical reasonableness (dreams, the demon) as a deliberate instrument — a solvent applied once, methodically, not a way of life.
evil demon
Genius malignus — the imagined supremely powerful deceiver of Meditation I; a meditative discipline for suspending even mathematical belief, and the remote ancestor of every brain-in-a-vat and simulation scenario since.
formal vs. objective reality
A thing's own degree of being (formal) versus the degree of being of what an idea represents (objective). The trademark argument runs on the principle that objective reality needs an adequate formal cause.
innate / adventitious / invented
The three sources of ideas: born with the mind (God, thought, extension), apparently received from without (heat, sound), or composed by me (hippogriffs).
real distinction
The relation between two substances each conceivable completely without the other — hence separable, at least by God's power. The conclusion of Meditation VI's dualism argument.
substance
What needs nothing else in order to exist — strictly, only God; by courtesy, minds and bodies, which need only God. (Spinoza took the strict reading and kept only the one substance.)
natural light
Lumen naturale — the intellect's native power to perceive self-evident truths (e.g., the causal principle), contrasted with mere "teachings of nature" like thirst, which aim at survival, not truth.
morale par provision
The provisional moral code of Discourse III — a temporary lodging for practical life while theoretical certainty is under reconstruction.
Cartesian circle
Arnauld's charge that clear and distinct perception and God's existence each certify the other. Descartes's reply distinguishes attended perception (self-guaranteeing while it lasts) from remembered demonstration (divinely guaranteed).
Larvatus prodeo — "I advance masked."
