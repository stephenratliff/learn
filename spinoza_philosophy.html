A Guide to a Philosopher

Spinoza

Substance, Necessity, and the Geometry of Freedom

Spinoza collapsed God into Nature, denied the universe any room for chance or free choice, and then wrote the whole system out as if it were Euclid — definitions, axioms, and propositions proved one from the next. Out of that same necessity he built an ethics: an account of the passions, of bondage to them, and of how understanding itself is the only freedom on offer.

Ethics, Part I — Of God

One substance, infinite attributes, endless modes

Descartes had left the world split into two independent kinds of stuff — thinking substance and extended substance — with God standing outside both as their creator. Spinoza's Ethics opens by dismantling that picture at the root. A substance, on his definition, is whatever exists in itself and is conceived through itself, needing nothing else to explain it. Once you accept that definition, he argues, you can show that two substances can never share a nature, that a substance can't be produced or limited by anything outside it, and that existence belongs to the very idea of substance the way having interior angles summing to 180° belongs to the idea of a triangle. Push the argument through and only one conclusion is left standing: there is exactly one substance, infinite, self-caused, and it is what people mean when they say "God."

Deus sive Natura — "God, or Nature." Not a personal creator who made the world and could have made it otherwise, but the world's own infinite, necessary being.

Everything else — every mind, every body, every thought, every stone — is not a separate thing alongside this substance but a mode of it: a way the one substance expresses itself, finite and dependent, the way a wave is a way the ocean is, rather than a second thing next to the ocean. Substance has infinitely many attributes, each expressing its essence completely in its own kind, but the finite human mind has access to only two: Extension (body, motion, space) and Thought (ideas, mind). A body and the idea of that body are, for Spinoza, one and the same mode expressed under two attributes — not two interacting things, which is how he dissolves Descartes' mind-body problem rather than solving it on Descartes' terms.

Producing nature and produced nature

Spinoza marks a distinction inside this single reality: natura naturans ("nature naturing") is substance considered as active and self-causing — God under the attributes, the productive power itself. Natura naturata ("nature natured") is everything that follows from that power — the whole infinite series of modes, finite and infinite, that necessarily flow from God's nature the way theorems flow from a definition. They are not two realities; they are one reality seen as cause and seen as effect.

The Geometric Method

Philosophy proved the way Euclid proved geometry

The full title is Ethics, Demonstrated in Geometrical Order, and Spinoza means that literally. Each of the five parts opens with a short list of definitions and axioms — claims meant to be self-evident or stipulated outright — and then proceeds through numbered propositions, each with a formal demonstration citing exactly which earlier definitions, axioms, or propositions it rests on. Many propositions carry corollaries (immediate consequences) and scholia (looser, more discursive remarks where Spinoza argues, mocks, or explains outside the formal apparatus).

The point of the style isn't decoration. If ethics and theology could be derived with the same rigor as geometry, then claims about God, the mind, and the good would carry the same kind of necessity as the claim that a triangle's angles sum to two right angles — not opinions to be debated by temperament, but consequences to be checked by proof. Nothing in the system is arbitrary and nothing is uncaused: for Spinoza, "necessary" and "true" end up meaning almost the same thing.

Nothing in nature is left open or up for grabs — every last thing exists and acts exactly as it does because God's nature could not have produced it any other way.

This is also where his rejection of free will lives. A choice that had no cause, Spinoza argues, is no more coherent than an uncaused stone. His own example: imagine a stone, set in motion, that became conscious partway through its flight. It would feel itself trying to keep moving, and — knowing nothing of the push that launched it — would swear it moved by free choice. Human beings are in exactly that position with respect to their own desires: aware of our wanting, blind to the chain of causes behind it, and so quick to call it freedom.

Ethics, Part III — Of the Affects

Everything strives to persevere in its being

Spinoza calls the drive at the center of Part III the conatus: each thing, so far as it can by its own power, strives to persist in existing. In a body this striving is largely unconscious; in a mind and body together it is desire — appetite that has become conscious of itself. Desire is, for Spinoza, our very essence, not one feeling among others.

From this single spring he derives the entire vocabulary of emotion mechanically, almost the way a chemist derives compounds from elements. Alongside desire there are only two other primitive affects: joy, the passage to a greater power of acting, and sadness, the passage to a lesser one. Every other named emotion — love, hate, hope, fear, pride, envy, pity — is joy or sadness combined with an idea about its cause.

On this analysis, love just is joy with a face attached to it — the feeling plus a belief about what caused it. Hatred is the same mechanism run on sadness instead.

Crucially, Spinoza treats the affects as facts of nature to be understood, not vices to be scolded — his stated aim in Part III is to study desire, joy, and sadness the way a geometer studies lines and planes, without weeping over them or condemning them first. Whether a given joy or sadness is a passion (something we suffer, arising from inadequate, confused ideas of external causes acting on us) or an action (something we do, following from our own adequate ideas) sets up the whole distinction between bondage and freedom that occupies the rest of the book.

Ethics, Parts IV–V — Bondage and Freedom

Three kinds of knowledge, and what freedom actually is

Part IV is bluntly titled "Of Human Bondage," and its argument is that most people spend their lives pushed around by passions — affects arising from confused, inadequate ideas of things outside their control. Because an inadequate idea gives only a partial, external view of its cause, the passions it produces are unstable and can conflict with each other and with our own good; the more of our mental life is made up of them, the less free we are, however much it feels like choosing.

Freedom, for Spinoza, is not an uncaused will — he's already ruled that out. It's the condition of acting from adequate ideas, understanding a thing's causes so fully that the idea becomes, in effect, self-explanatory and internally caused, the way a geometric proof is once you grasp it. He sorts all knowledge into three ascending kinds.

To the exact degree a mind runs on the second and third kinds of knowledge instead of the first, the passions simply have nothing left to grab onto.

The third kind, intuitive knowledge, is not mysticism in the usual sense — it's the limit case of understanding, where a particular thing is grasped directly through the essence of God's attributes rather than pieced together from general rules. Spinoza's phrase for the vantage this gives is sub specie aeternitatis, "under a certain aspect of eternity": seeing a thing as it necessarily follows from the whole of nature, rather than as a passing event tangled up with your own hopes and fears about it.

Ethics, Part V — Of Human Freedom

Blessedness is not the wage of virtue — it is virtue

The Ethics ends where its geometry has been pointing the whole time. Understanding things through the third kind of knowledge — grasping them sub specie aeternitatis, as necessary expressions of God's own nature — produces, Spinoza says, a distinctive joy: the mind's love for the very cause it now understands itself to be an expression of. He calls this the intellectual love of God (amor Dei intellectualis), and argues it is strictly part of the infinite love with which God, or Nature, loves itself. Human blessedness turns out to be a share in that self-relation, not a private reward handed down for good behavior.

This lets Spinoza invert the ordinary picture of virtue and happiness. We don't first control our passions through effort and get blessedness as a prize afterward; the clear, adequate understanding just is the state of being blessed, and the reduced grip of the passions is simply what that understanding looks like from the inside. Virtue and blessedness are the same fact considered from two angles — one more reason he insists that the strenuous, guilt-driven picture of ethics as obedience to command has always been the wrong picture.

Someone who has actually reached this understanding is not spared misfortune, but is no longer at its mercy — grasping himself, God, and things under this same necessity, he holds onto something hardship can't take back from him.

None of this promises personal immortality in any ordinary sense — Spinoza is explicit that the imaginative, memory-laden self doesn't survive the body — but he does hold that something of the mind, the part constituted by adequate and eternal ideas, is not touched by time at all, because adequate ideas were never "in time" the way passing sensations are. That claim is one of the most contested in the whole book, and commentators remain divided on how literally to take it.

Theological-Political Treatise, 1670 & Political Treatise, unfinished

A naturalistic state, and a defense of the right to think

Spinoza's political writing applies the same naturalism to scripture and to the state that Part I applies to God. The Theological-Political Treatise reads the Bible historically and philologically — as a human document shaped by the circumstances, language, and limited understanding of the people who wrote it — rather than as a source of literal physics or metaphysics. Its lasting core, on his reading, is a simple practical morality: justice and charity toward others. Everything else in it is negotiable in a way theologians of his day found intolerable, and the book was banned in several jurisdictions.

His account of political authority is built the same way his metaphysics is: from power, not from divine right or an antecedent moral law. A state's right extends exactly as far as its actual collective power to enforce it; a government that governs so badly that it provokes constant rebellion has, in effect, lost the power that was its right in the first place. He argues from this basis for broad toleration and freedom of speech and philosophical inquiry, not as a moral nicety but because suppressing thought is both unenforceable and corrosive to the very stability rulers want.

The unfinished Political Treatise extends the analysis to compare monarchy, aristocracy, and democracy as institutional designs for containing power with power, and speaks favorably of democracy as the form that keeps sovereignty closest to the collective body of citizens who actually hold it — though he did not live to complete the chapter on democracy itself.

His most quoted methodological line, closely paraphrased: study human actions and desires the way you would study lines, planes, and solids — aim to understand them, rather than to laugh at them, weep over them, or curse them.

Legacy

What changed because of this work

In his lifetime and for a century after, Spinoza was mostly a name to be denounced — "Spinozism" was shorthand for atheism, and his books circulated as much through underground copying and covers of other titles as through open sale. The German Idealists changed that: Herder and the young Goethe read him as a poet of nature rather than an atheist, and by the time Coleridge and the Romantics took him up he had become, in Novalis's famous phrase, "God-intoxicated" rather than godless. Hegel and Schelling both built substantial parts of their systems in argument with him.

Later readings have run in different directions again: the 20th century saw him claimed as a forerunner of biblical criticism, of Enlightenment secular politics, and — through Gilles Deleuze's influential studies — as a philosopher of immanence and affect newly relevant to continental philosophy. His mechanistic account of the emotions has drawn renewed attention from neuroscientists such as Antonio Damasio, who has argued that Spinoza anticipated genuine insights about how emotion and cognition are physically intertwined.

The 1656 cherem was never lifted in his lifetime, and a proposal to formally revisit it at Amsterdam's Portuguese Synagogue was considered and ultimately declined in the 2010s — a small sign of how unsettled his reception still is, more than three centuries on.
